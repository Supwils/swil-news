## I. Defense & Security: Frontier AI on Classified Networks

### 1. U.S. agreements to deploy commercial AI on classified networks; Anthropic not on the list
**Summary:** The Associated Press, citing the Defense Department, reports that the U.S. military has reached agreements with seven technology companies—Google, Microsoft, Amazon Web Services, Nvidia, OpenAI, Reflection, and SpaceX—to use their artificial intelligence capabilities on classified computer networks to support warfighter decision-making in complex environments, with expanded use through platforms such as GenAI.mil. Reporting from the same day indicates the vendor set was later expanded to eight firms, including Oracle, reflecting a deliberate mix of proprietary frontier models and open-source-oriented options to avoid over-reliance on a single provider. Anthropic does not appear among the partners, against the backdrop of public disputes and litigation with the administration over permissible military use, contract terms, and “supply chain risk” designations.

### 2. China orders Meta to unwind Manus acquisition: cross-border AI deals and security review
**Summary:** Reuters reported on April 27 that China’s National Development and Reform Commission, through its foreign-investment security review office, disclosed measures prohibiting foreign investment in Manus and requiring parties to withdraw the acquisition transaction, following Meta’s more than $2 billion purchase of the AI-agent-focused startup. Coverage notes Manus had restructured toward Singapore to navigate U.S. and Chinese investment constraints, yet regulators still emphasize technology origin, core R&D, and founding-team ties as review hooks—widely interpreted as raising the compliance bar for “Singapore window”-style restructurings.

### 4. Connecticut House passes sweeping AI accountability and transparency legislation (SB 5)
**Summary:** CT Mirror reports that on May 1 the Connecticut House passed Senate Bill 5—the Connecticut Artificial Intelligence Responsibility and Transparency Act—by a 131–17 vote and sent it to the governor. The measure spans AI in employment decisions, AI programs in state agencies, and protections around minors’ interactions with social media and AI chatbots, alongside workforce development, literacy, and a state regulatory sandbox. The governor’s office indicated intent to sign, while reporting notes Trump administration efforts to steer states toward a single federal standard rather than patchworks.

### 5. Fed Vice Chair for Supervision: traditional model-risk guidance doesn’t map to generative or agentic AI
**Summary:** The Federal Reserve published remarks by Vice Chair for Supervision Michelle W. Bowman from a Financial Stability Oversight Council AI roundtable on May 1. She explains that—in coordination with the OCC and FDIC—model risk management supervision (SR letter 26-2) has been narrowed so legacy model-risk guidance does **not** apply to generative or agentic AI, avoiding misfit rules on fast-evolving technology; third-party risk guidance also needs sharpening. Bowman cites Anthropic’s Mythos model as emblematic of cyber capabilities that can help or harm, depending on use.
**Links:**
- [Federal Reserve Board — Speech: Artificial Intelligence in the Financial System (Bowman)](https://www.federalreserve.gov/newsevents/speech/bowman20260501a.htm)
**Commentary:** Bank supervision is differentiating statistical models from generative/agentic stacks—creating space for adoption while signaling that governance tooling remains a work in progress.
