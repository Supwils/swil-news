## II. Biomedicine & Health

### 3. First fully AI-designed vaccine active enters humans: Cambridge/DIOSynVax pan-Sarbecovirus pEVAC-PS Phase 1 safe
**Summary:** ScienceDaily and pharmaphorum (June 5, 2026) report that University of Cambridge and spinout DIOSynVax completed a Phase 1 trial of pEVAC-PS, published in the Journal of Infection (DOI: 10.1016/j.jinf.2026.106759). Thirty-nine healthy volunteers aged 18–50 who had already received two or three COVID-19 doses received needle-free DNA jet delivery (0.2–1.2 mg dose escalation on Days 0 and 28) at NIHR sites in Cambridge and Southampton with no serious adverse events. Immunogenicity was “modest but variable,” with measurable cross-reactivity to SARS-CoV-2, SARS-CoV-1, and bat Sarbecoviruses not yet in humans. This is the first human test of a vaccine whose active ingredient was created entirely through computer simulation; a larger Phase 2 is planned.
**Links:**

- [Journal of Infection — A phase I trial of pEVAC-PS (Vol 92, Issue 6)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/journal/journal-of-infection/vol/92/issue/6)

**Commentary:** Human safety validates the broad-design concept, but limited neutralization means the AI pipeline still faces hard Phase 2/3 bars before it can claim pandemic-proof protection.

### 4. Cervical epidural spinal cord stimulation: ~32% arm-strength gain in chronic post-stroke hemiparesis
**Summary:** Nature Medicine, a Carnegie Mellon news release, and EurekAlert (June 4, 2026) report final results of a feasibility trial: seven participants with severe chronic upper-limb deficits (FMA 15–35) received unilateral cervical epidural leads for four weeks with only ~8.6 hours of movement training (~5.5 hours with stimulation on). When stimulation was active, arm strength rose ~32% on average (+5.6 FMA points); all seven showed reduced spasticity, and three with residual corticospinal finger connectivity gained hand movement. No serious adverse events occurred; gains faded when stimulation stopped, suggesting an assistive neuroprosthetic rather than short-term rehab. ClinicalTrials.gov: NCT04512690; DOI: 10.1038/s41591-026-04435-1.
**Links:**
- [Nature Medicine — Spinal cord stimulation for upper limb motor function in chronic post-stroke hemiparesis](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-026-04435-1)
- [Carnegie Mellon — Spinal cord stimulation restores movement years after stroke](https://engineering.cmu.edu/news-events/news/2026/06/04-spinal-cord.html)

**Commentary:** Repurposing pain-focused SCS hardware for upper-limb motor restoration opens an implantable path for millions still disabled years after stroke, pending larger trials of durable home benefit.

### 5. Natural depsipeptide manikomycin: first antibiotic targeting the bacterial ribosome E-site
**Summary:** A Nature paper (June 3, 2026) from McMaster’s Gerry Wright group used improved fractionation to isolate manikomycin—a minor product from the well-studied Streptomyces rimosus strain that produces oxytetracycline. The cyclic depsipeptide kills multidrug-resistant Enterobacteriaceae without cross-resistance to major clinical classes. Biochemistry and cryo-EM show binding at the large-subunit E-site, blocking 3′ tRNA entry and inhibiting translocation—the first known antibacterial agent on this target. Low human-cell toxicity is reported; ~60 derivatives are being optimized (DOI: 10.1038/s41586-026-10589-2).
**Links:**
- [Nature — A natural depsipeptide antibiotic binds the E-site of the bacterial ribosome](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10589-2)
- [GEN — New Antibiotic, Manikomycin, Acts on Novel Ribosomal Target](https://www.genengnews.com/topics/infectious-diseases/new-antibiotic-manikomycin-acts-on-novel-ribosomal-target/)

**Commentary:** Improved fractionation still yields novel scaffolds from “mined-out” actinomycetes—a reusable playbook as the resistance crisis deepens.

### 7. ISS EMIT mineral maps cut dust iron-oxide radiative uncertainty by more than sixfold
**Summary:** Cornell Chronicle and Phys.org (June 1, 2026; widely cited through June 5) describe a Cornell-led team using NASA’s EMIT mission aboard the ISS to constrain how airborne mineral dust interacts with sunlight. Iron-oxide-related uncertainty fell from 0.62 W/m² to 0.1 W/m² (>6×); over the Sahara, model errors dropped up to ~80%. Published in Nature Geoscience (DOI: 10.1038/s41561-026-01996-1), the work shifts focus toward dust transport, particle size, and evolving sources—not just unknown composition.
**Links:**
- [Cornell Chronicle — Study clarifies how atmospheric dust shapes climate](https://news.cornell.edu/stories/2026/06/study-clarifies-how-atmospheric-dust-shapes-climate)

**Commentary:** Observable mineral constraints move the dust warming/cooling debate from assumed composition toward testable transport physics—critical for Sahara–Atlantic–Amazon teleconnections under warming.

### 8. Global analysis: land carbon uptake gains driven by water-use efficiency and canopy expansion, not photosynthetic optimum temperature shift
**Summary:** Phys.org (June 3, 2026) covers a One Earth study finding that rising maximum terrestrial carbon uptake in recent decades owes <20% to shifts in photosynthetic optimum temperature; dominant drivers are greater leaf area and higher carbon fixed per unit water, including in humid tropical, temperate, and cold regions. Drier air and soils further weaken temperature-adaptation signals (DOI: 10.1016/j.oneear.2026.101703). Authors argue next-generation carbon-cycle models must couple hydraulic strategy and canopy dynamics—not temperature parameters alone.
**Links:**

**Commentary:** If the land carbon “bonus” reflects water reallocation rather than thermal adaptation, drought years could reverse recent uptake faster than net-zero roadmaps assume.

## IV. Fundamental Research

### 10. Queen-bee development shaped by worker-built wax architecture—not royal jelly alone
**Summary:** Science News and Phys.org (June 3, 2026) describe a Nature paper showing that in western and eastern honeybees, workers engineer softer, less dense, chemically distinct “royal cribs” with elevated temperature; larvae under worker wax caps fare worse than under queen wax even on identical royal jelly. Queens emerge in ~16 days versus ~21 for workers—an advantage when colonies urgently need a new ruler (DOI: 10.1038/s41586-026-10534-3).
**Links:**
- [Science News — A secret to making a queen bee may lie in the wax around it](https://www.sciencenews.org/article/queen-honeybee-development-wax)

**Commentary:** The finding revises textbook “royal jelly makes the queen” lore, showing social insects engineer developmental microenvironments through specialized division of labor.
