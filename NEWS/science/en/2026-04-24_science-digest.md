### 3. NASA and ESA Heliophysics Spacecraft Jointly Record Kreutz Sungrazer Comet Disintegrating Near Perihelion
**Summary:** NASA Science's "The Sun Spot" blog reported on April 16, 2026 that comet C/2026 A1 (MAPS) disintegrated while making a close pass of the Sun. NASA and ESA's SOHO and STEREO instruments captured imaging and coronagraph data, providing new observational samples for studying the structure, fragmentation, and dust dynamics of Kreutz family sungrazers. Such events couple cometary mass transport with the coronal magnetic field environment in a single observational window, serving as natural experiments for testing comet models and space weather effects.
**Links:**
- [NASA Science — NASA Heliophysics Spacecraft Witness Comet's Demise](https://science.nasa.gov/blogs/the-sun-spot/2026/04/16/nasa-heliophysics-spacecraft-witness-comets-demise/)
**Commentary:** Sungrazer disintegrations are "free" high-time-resolution plasma and dust laboratories — their value lies in compressing near-perihelion physics that would be otherwise difficult to replicate on the ground into the observational geometry of an existing spacecraft fleet.

### 5. JPL Physicist Turyshev Reviews: Bridging Cosmological-Scale "Disconnect" and Dark Energy Tests with Solar System Precision Experiments
**Summary:** Around April 24, 2026, multiple science media outlets relayed perspectives from NASA Jet Propulsion Laboratory physicist **Slava Turyshev** and his *Physical Review D* review work. At galaxy scales and beyond, phenomena like accelerating cosmic expansion suggest dark energy or modified gravity; within the solar system's strong-field, high-density environment, General Relativity predictions align closely with tracking and ranging data — the so-called "cosmological–local physics disconnect." The discussion covers how **chameleon** and **Vainshtein screening** mechanisms may suppress locally detectable signals, and emphasizes the need to derive **falsifiable predictions testable in the solar system** from cosmological survey constraints (Euclid, DESI), then design next-generation precision missions accordingly — otherwise repeat local tests offer diminishing marginal returns.
