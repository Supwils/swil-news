## I. Exoplanets, Astronomical Big Data, and Mission Synergy

### 1. University of Warwick debuts the end-to-end AI pipeline **RAVEN** on **NASA TESS**’ first four years: **118 newly validated exoplanets** (including **31** newly detected worlds) and **>2,000** high-quality candidates (nearly **1,000** brand-new) (exoplanet “demographics”)
**Summary:** **ScienceDaily (May 3, 2026; University of Warwick materials)** and the **Warwick press release** report that the team applied **RAVEN** to **>2.2 million stars** in **TESS–SPOC full-frame images**, focusing on close-in planets with **orbital periods under 16 days**, and compressed detection, machine-learning vetting, and statistical validation into one workflow. The sample includes **ultra-short-period (<24 h)** planets, rare **“Neptunian desert”** worlds, and newly characterized **compact multi-planet systems**. A companion **MNRAS** demographics paper quotes about **9–10%** of **FGK dwarf** stars hosting a close-in planet (uncertainties up to roughly an order of magnitude tighter than legacy **Kepler**-era pictures in places) and a first direct prevalence estimate for **Neptunian-desert planets** near **0.08%** of Sun-like hosts. Results appear in **MNRAS** (e.g., **DOI 10.1093/mnras/stag512**, **10.1093/mnras/stag022**), and interactive catalogs aim to feed follow-up with **ground-based telescopes** and **ESA PLATO**. (Population definitions follow the papers.)
**Links:**
- [ScienceDaily — Powerful AI finds 100+ hidden planets in NASA data including rare and extreme worlds](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/05/260502233926.htm)
- [University of Warwick — AI approach uncovers dozens of hidden planets in TESS data](https://warwick.ac.uk/news/pressreleases/ai-approach-uncovers-dozens-of-hidden-planets/)
**Commentary:** Planet surveys drowning in **candidates** need **auditable throughput**; packaging **simulation-trained AI, vetting, and debiasing** is how **TESS-class archives** become demographic instruments—not anecdotal collections.

## IV. Nanoscale Electronics and Global Ecological Risk

### 8. **Science Tokyo** demonstrates a **~25 nm** **ferroelectric tunnel junction (FTJ)** memory cell: **performance improves as the device shrinks**, pointing to low-power **wearables / edge AI**
**Summary:** **ScienceDaily (May 3, 2026; Science Tokyo materials)** and an **English institute article (Apr 28, 2026)** describe **Yutaka Majima**’s team leveraging **hafnia-type ferroelectricity**, **semicircular electrode shaping via thermal treatment**, and deliberate **extreme downscaling** to curb **grain-boundary leakage**, achieving **better switching as size decreases**—counter to a common scaling intuition. Use cases sketched include **long-lived wearables**, **low-maintenance IoT sensing**, and **energy-frugal AI memory hierarchies**, emphasizing **CMOS compatibility**. (Fab insert still demands **reliability, yield, and array architecture** proof.)
**Links:**
- [ScienceDaily — Scientists built a memory chip that breaks the rules of miniaturization](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/05/260502233908.htm)
- [Science Tokyo — Smaller but stronger: a 25-nanometer memory that challenges traditional limits](https://www.isct.ac.jp/en/news/zchajorhi75l)
**Commentary:** Reliable **FTJ arrays** would matter not only for **power**—they could widen the thermal budget for **near-sensor inference** and **in-memory-ish** compute paths.

### 9. **Land vertebrates** face expanding **multi-hazard extreme-event exposure** this century (global-change biology)
**Summary:** A **Nature Ecology & Evolution** paper linked from **Carbon Brief *DeBriefed*** uses **ISIMIP** climate-impact simulations and range maps for **33,936 terrestrial vertebrates** to project changes in exposure to **drought, heatwaves, river floods, and wildfire**. Under the medium–high **SSP3–7.0** storyline, by **2050** an average **74%** of the area within species’ current ranges may be exposed to **heatwaves**, **16%** to **wildfires**, **8%** to **droughts**, and **3%** to **river floods**; by **2085**, **36%** of range area may face **two or more** extreme types, with **236** ecoregions exceeding **50%** area exposed to **≥2** hazards (**2085**, **SSP3–7.0**; definitions/thresholds per **Methods**). The paper stresses **exposure ≠ impact** and notes limits such as **small islands** and static ranges (**Discussion**). (Pairing with **protected areas, corridors, and demography** reframes management.)
