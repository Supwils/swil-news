## I. Fundamental Research & Particle Physics

### 1. Large Hadron Collider Enters Third Long Shutdown Today: Four-Year Upgrade Toward HL-LHC
**Summary:** According to CERN's official schedule, phys.org, and New Scientist (June 27–29, 2026), the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) at CERN entered its third long shutdown (LS3) on June 29, 2026, lasting approximately 47 months (beam-to-beam), with restart as the High-Luminosity LHC (HL-LHC) planned around June 2030. During the shutdown, sections of the accelerator will be dismantled and replaced with new quadrupole magnets, cryogenic systems, and other equipment to boost collision luminosity by roughly an order of magnitude, enabling more precise Higgs boson studies and searches for rare phenomena such as dark matter. HL-LHC project leaders expect roughly 380 million Higgs bosons over the upgraded machine's lifetime, versus about 55 million accumulated since operations began in 2008. New CERN Director-General Mark Thomson, who took office on January 1, 2026, is overseeing this transition.

### 2. Physicists Realize "Fractional Fermi Sea" in Ultracold Cesium: A New Critical Phase in 1D Quantum Matter
**Summary:** According to ScienceDaily, phys.org, and The Quantum Insider (June 29, 2026), Hanns-Christoph Nägerl's team at the University of Innsbruck, with theory collaborator Alvise Bastianello (CNRS / Université Paris-Dauphine), drove a one-dimensional ultracold cesium gas into a highly excited yet highly ordered "fractional Fermi sea" by cyclically switching particle interactions between strongly repulsive and strongly attractive regimes. Particles follow a "reduced occupancy" rule; the work is published in *Physical Review Letters* (DOI: 10.1103/j3s5-gjpf). The state shows Friedel oscillations and correlation functions incompatible with Tomonaga-Luttinger liquid theory, pointing to an exotic critical phase beyond established paradigms and opening new routes for cold-atom quantum simulation of universal quantum behavior.
**Links:**
- [ScienceDaily — Physicists create a strange new quantum state called a fractional Fermi sea](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/06/260619101330.htm)
- [phys.org — Out-of-equilibrium cesium atoms reveal fractional Fermi seas](https://phys.org/news/2026-06-equilibrium-cesium-atoms-reveal-fractional.html)

**Commentary:** Cold-atom simulators are moving from "reproducing known models" to "creating new phases of matter" — the fractional Fermi sea shows that nonequilibrium driving can stabilize quantum critical states absent from textbooks.

## II. Paleontology & Planetary Science

### 3. Antarctica's First Dinosaur Fossil Confirmed: 1985 Titanosaur Tail Vertebra Sat in a Drawer for 40 Years
**Summary:** Per BBC, the British Antarctic Survey (BAS), and the Natural History Museum (June 29, 2026), a tail vertebra collected by BAS geologist Mike Thomson on James Ross Island in 1985 has been formally identified as an anterior caudal vertebra of a titanosaur sauropod — the first dinosaur bone ever found on the Antarctic continent. The paper appears in *Acta Palaeontologica Polonica*. The specimen is about 10 cm long; size comparisons suggest an individual roughly 6–7 m (23 ft) long that lived about 82 million years ago in the lower Campanian of the Late Cretaceous, when Antarctica was forested. The find revises the record previously credited to a 1986 Argentine ankylosaur discovery and adds a key piece to Late Cretaceous Gondwanan dinosaur dispersal.
**Links:**
- [BBC — Antarctica's first ever dinosaur bone discovered in a drawer](https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/articles/cdep67jnn9zo)
- [British Antarctic Survey — Antarctica's first dinosaur fossil confirmed from 1985 Antarctic expedition](https://www.bas.ac.uk/news/antarcticas-first-dinosaur-fossil-confirmed-from-1985-antarctic-expedition/)

**Commentary:** A "large reptile vertebra" in a museum drawer rewrote a continent's paleontological timeline — with dinosaur evidence on Antarctica still scarce, every formally identified bone is precious.

## III. Space & Deep Space

### 4. NASA Swift Orbit-Boost Mission Enters Final 24 Hours: LINK Robotic Servicer No Earlier Than June 30
**Summary:** Per NASA Science and phys.org (June 2026 follow-up), NASA and Katalyst Space Technologies' Neil Gehrels Swift Observatory orbit-boost mission is ready for launch no earlier than June 30 at 6:23 a.m. EDT (22:23 UTC+12) from Kwajalein Atoll aboard a Northrop Grumman Pegasus XL air-launched rocket. Katalyst's LINK robotic servicer will spend weeks approaching and capturing Swift, then over months raise its orbit from about 224 miles (360 km) to about 373 miles (600 km), preventing reentry later in 2026. Swift's decay accelerated unexpectedly due to heightened solar-driven atmospheric drag; success would set a precedent for commercial in-orbit servicing to extend aging observatories such as Hubble. Stargazer and Pegasus XL arrived at Kwajalein on June 18.

### 5. Viatris Reports Positive Phase 3 Top-Line for VR-205 in Japanese IgA Nephropathy: NDA Targeted by End of 2026
**Summary:** Per a Viatris announcement on June 29, 2026, targeted-release budesonide VR-205 (Nefecon®) met its primary endpoint in a Phase 3 trial in Japanese adults with primary immunoglobulin A nephropathy (IgAN): at 9 months, geometric mean urine protein-to-creatinine ratio (UPCR) fell 33.75% from baseline (95% CI: -45.27 to -19.80; p < 0.001), with statistically significant and clinically meaningful UPCR improvements at 6 and 12 months, improved estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR), and reductions in serum creatinine and urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio (UACR). Safety over nine months was consistent with the global Phase 3 program; no patients progressed to dialysis, transplant, or severe renal impairment. The company targets a Japanese New Drug Application submission by end of 2026.

### 7. *Nature Neuroscience*: Foamy Microglia Linked to Rapid MS Progression; MAGL Emerges as Potential Target
**Summary:** Per ScienceDaily (June 29, 2026) and a *Nature Neuroscience* paper (van der Vliet et al., DOI: 10.1038/s41593-026-02302-3), teams from the Netherlands Institute for Neuroscience, Leiden University, and Utrecht University identified foamy GPNMB⁺ microglia/macrophages in postmortem secondary progressive MS brain tissue associated with lesion expansion and accelerated disability. After clearing damaged myelin, these cells accumulate lipid droplets and lysosomal stress, enriching oxylipins, cholesterol esters, and other lipid-metabolism abnormalities. Monoacylglycerol lipase (MAGL) is elevated in lesions containing foamy cells; MAGL inhibition promoted lesion recovery and reduced microgliosis in a demyelination mouse model. Cerebrospinal fluid oxylipin levels correlate with foamy-lesion proportion and may serve as progression biomarkers; Roche's MAGL inhibitor RO7268489 plus ocrelizumab Phase 2 trial (Mintaka, NCT07282574) is recruiting.
**Links:**

### 8. *eBioMedicine* Two-Year RCT: High-Dose DHA Reaches the Brain but Fails to Improve Cognition or Hippocampal Atrophy
**Summary:** Per ScienceDaily (June 29, 2026) and an *eBioMedicine* paper (Yassine et al., DOI: 10.1016/j.ebiom.2026.106316), USC Keck School of Medicine researchers randomized 365 adults aged 55–80 with low dietary omega-3 intake and dementia risk factors to 2,000 mg/day DHA or placebo for two years. At 6 months, cerebrospinal fluid DHA rose 17% on average and red-blood-cell omega-3 index climbed from 4.9% to 11%, confirming central nervous system target engagement; yet at 24 months there were no significant differences in memory, cognitive tests, or hippocampal volume versus placebo, including in APOE ε4 carriers. Authors stress that high-dose supplements alone cannot substitute for overall brain-healthy lifestyles.
**Links:**
- [ScienceDaily — Millions take omega-3 fish oil for brain health but a new study found no benefit](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/06/260622014300.htm)

**Commentary:** "Gets into the brain" ≠ "prevents disease" — with delivery bottlenecks ruled out, dementia-prevention narratives must return from pills to integrated diet, exercise, and metabolic-health interventions.

### 9. Western European June 2026 Heatwave Continues: WWA Finds 45% of Cities Broke Indoor WBGT Thresholds June 18–29
**Summary:** Per World Weather Attribution updates (June 26–29, 2026) and UN News follow-up, the second major Western European heatwave since June 17 persisted through June 29, with France, Germany, Italy, Spain, and southern England 5–12°C above seasonal norms. WWA rapid attribution concludes that at roughly 1.4°C global warming, similar June heat would have been "virtually impossible" in 1976; nighttime extremes are about 100 times more likely than in 2003. Analysis of wet-bulb globe temperature (WBGT) across 854 European cities for June 18–29 found 45% breaking or nearing indoor WBGT thresholds — especially dangerous when heat combines with high humidity. WHO said on June 28 that Europe recorded more than 1,300 excess deaths linked to high temperatures since June 21; a North Atlantic "cold blob" and persistent high-pressure heat dome jointly shaped the event.

### 11. *Nature Communications*: Perovskite Hydroxyl Nanocavities Enable Near-Barrierless Hydrogen Transfer for Nitrate Electroreduction to Ammonia
**Summary:** Per a *Nature Communications* paper published June 29, 2026 (Xu et al., DOI: 10.1038/s41467-026-74405-1), researchers built hydroxyl-enriched nanocavities on La₀.₄Sr₀.₆FeO₃₋δ perovskite via electrochemical reduction-assisted selective Sr leaching, enabling near-barrierless intramolecular hydrogen-bond hydrogen transfer. The catalyst achieved 97.81% Faradaic efficiency and 51.37 mg h⁻¹ cm⁻² ammonia yield at -0.8 V vs. RHE, outperforming cavity-free controls and ranking among top NITRR catalysts; a renewable-energy-powered electrolyzer reached ampere-level current at 2.23 V cell voltage. Techno-economic analysis highlights dual benefits for ammonia synthesis economics and nitrate remediation.

### 12. Texas A&M TRIP Raman Technique Directly Measures Quantum Forces in Protein–Drug Interactions
**Summary:** Per The Quantum Insider (June 29, 2026), Texas A&M University's Institute for Quantum Science and Engineering invented Thermostable Raman Interaction Profiling (TRIP), firing a laser on samples and recording vibrational signals to directly measure quantum forces such as π–π stacking in proteins and drug-binding effects. On SARS-CoV-2 main protease Mpro, TRIP predicted antiviral drug binding potency consistent with known efficacy rankings. Authors believe TRIP could accelerate drug discovery in cancer, Alzheimer's, and infectious disease, serving as a "visibility amplifier" for expert inspection rather than an autonomous reading system.
**Links:**
- [The Quantum Insider — Texas A&M Researchers Develop Quantum Technique for Drug Discovery](https://thequantuminsider.com/2026/06/29/quantum-technique-drug-discovery/)

**Commentary:** Drug design moves from structure prediction toward "force spectroscopy" — TRIP turns hard-to-compute weak interactions into experimentally readable vibrational fingerprints, shortening lead-validation loops.
