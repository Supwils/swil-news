### 2. ISS Zvezda Module Leak Worsens; NASA Orders Safe Haven Before Russia Pauses High-Risk Structural Repair
**Summary:** Per NASA's space station blog (June 5, 2026) and ScienceDaily (June 8), cracks in the Zvezda service module's transfer tunnel (PrK) have caused small atmosphere leaks since 2019. During Progress 95 cargo operations the week of June 1, Roscosmos measured the leak rate rising to about two pounds per day and identified new suspected leak areas. On June 5, Russia planned to cut a bracket for broader inspection; NASA judged the procedure could elevate structural risk and directed the four SpaceX Crew-12 members plus NASA astronaut Chris Williams (Soyuz MS-28) into a safe-haven posture inside the Dragon spacecraft. Roscosmos paused structural work that morning for additional measurements; the crew later resumed normal operations. A lasting fix remains under discussion.

### 3. Katalyst LINK On-Orbit Servicing Spacecraft Arrives at Wallops; Planned Launch This Month to Raise Swift's Orbit
**Summary:** Per WRAL in June 2026, Arizona startup Katalyst Space Technologies' LINK robotic life-extension spacecraft has arrived at NASA's Wallops Flight Facility in Virginia for final integration with a Northrop Grumman Pegasus XL rocket, with launch planned later this month. If successful, LINK will autonomously rendezvous with and capture the in-orbit Swift gamma-ray observatory, then use its own propulsion to raise Swift to a safer altitude and extend its science mission. The flight is among the first high-risk, high-reward U.S. government-funded on-orbit servicing demonstrations; NASA has expressed interest in extending other aging but scientifically productive missions such as Hubble, and the U.S. Department of Defense also tracks such technology for military satellite life extension.

### 5. SLAMF6 Identified as Druggable T-Cell Immune Brake; Nature Reports Cis Homotypic Suppression of Anti-Tumor Immunity
**Summary:** Per ScienceDaily on June 9, 2026, and a Nature paper (DOI: 10.1038/s41586-026-10106-5, published February 11, 2026), Université de Montréal's André Veillette team found that SLAMF6 self-activates through cis homotypic interactions on the T-cell surface, sending inhibitory signals that weaken anti-tumor immunity independently of tumor SLAMF6 expression. Monoclonal antibodies blocking these cis interactions enhanced human T-cell activation, reduced exhausted T-cell fractions, and showed strong anti-tumor effects in mice — outperforming existing SLAMF6-targeting tools. Researchers believe the antibodies could offer a new checkpoint option for patients who no longer respond to PD-1/PD-L1 therapy, with early clinical trials planned.
**Links:**
- [ScienceDaily — The secret reason some cancer treatments stop working](http://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/06/260608040021.htm)
- [Nature — SLAMF6 as a drug-targetable suppressor of T cell immunity against cancer](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10106-5)

**Commentary:** A cell-autonomous checkpoint that does not require tumor ligands broadens immunotherapy reach — but human safety and efficacy remain to be proven.

### 6. ETH "Compound 10" Blocks GRK2 Aggregation, Slowing Neurodegeneration in Alzheimer's Mouse Models
**Summary:** Per ETH Zurich's June 2026 news release and ScienceDaily (June 8), Professor Ursula Quitterer's team reports in Cell Reports Medicine (DOI: 10.1016/j.xcrm.2026.102707) that the enzyme GRK2 becomes inactivated and aggregates in dementia patient brain tissue, damaging mitochondria and promoting amyloid-beta deposition in a pathological feedback loop. "Compound 10" prevents GRK2 aggregation; in mouse models it improves mitochondrial function, lowers amyloid-beta deposits, delays nerve-cell death, and extends survival, with anti-aging-related effects in 1.5–2-year-old mice. ETH has filed a patent and is seeking industry partners for preclinical development.

## III. Climate, Energy & Environment

### 8. CFC Replacements Linked to 335,000+ Tonnes of Persistent TFA "Forever Chemical" Deposition
**Summary:** Per ScienceDaily on June 9, 2026, Lancaster University's Ryan Hossaini et al. estimate in Geophysical Research Letters (DOI: 10.1029/2025GL119216) that CFC-replacement refrigerants and certain anesthetic gases deposited roughly 335,500 tonnes of trifluoroacetic acid (TFA) onto Earth's surface between 2000 and 2022. TFA is highly persistent and has been detected in rainwater and remote Arctic ice; because some replacement gases remain in the atmosphere for decades, annual TFA production is expected to peak sometime between 2025 and 2100 and continue rising. The study finds nearly all Arctic TFA originates from CFC replacements despite the region's distance from major emission sources.
**Links:**
- [ScienceDaily — An invisible forever chemical rain is falling across the planet](http://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/06/260609025509.htm)
- [Geophysical Research Letters — Growth in Production and Environmental Deposition of Trifluoroacetic Acid](https://doi.org/10.1029/2025GL119216)

**Commentary:** An unintended cost of ozone-layer repair — incorporating "green replacements" into full lifecycle monitoring is unavoidable for next-generation fluorochemical regulation.

### 9. Terrestrial Nitrogen Limitation Weakens Carbon-Sink Feedback; Extra 0.16–0.25°C Warming by 2100
**Summary:** Per Communications Earth & Environment (June 8, 2026; DOI: 10.1038/s43247-026-03736-0), Tang et al. use the MAGICC-CNit emulator of CMIP6 Earth system models for emission-driven probabilistic projections quantifying nitrogen's role in fully coupled climate–carbon–nitrogen dynamics: nitrogen limitation weakens the carbon-concentration feedback by 37% (1.10±0.36 to 0.70±0.37 GtC ppm⁻¹) and the carbon-climate feedback by 16%, reducing land carbon uptake by 18%–54%, raising atmospheric CO₂ by 15–57 ppm and global warming by an additional 0.16–0.25°C by 2100; high-emission scenarios risk land becoming a net carbon source. Carbon-only ESMs cannot capture future nitrogen constraints.

## IV. Fundamental Research

### 12. MIT 3D-Prints Triaxial Electrospray Arrays to Mass-Produce Three-Layer Drug-Delivery Microparticles in Hours
**Summary:** Per MIT News on June 9, 2026, Luis Fernando Velásquez-García's team at MIT's Microsystems Technology Laboratories used vat photopolymerization 3D printing to fabricate triaxial electrospray emitter arrays with 16 nozzles in about one square centimeter, completing complex devices in a few hours without semiconductor cleanroom microfabrication. Three-dimensional microchannels uniformly supply liquid; under electric fields the devices generate stable three-layer (core-shell-shell) microdroplets that solidify into layered microparticles for timed drug release, self-healing materials, or three-layer biosensing particles. Flow rates and voltages precisely tune each layer's thickness. The work appears in Virtual and Physical Prototyping.

### 13. IonQ Trapped-Ion Quantum Computer Achieves First Fermionic ν=1/3 Laughlin Topological State
**Summary:** Per Nature Communications (June 8, 2026; DOI: 10.1038/s41467-026-72769-y), researchers prepared the fermionic filling factor ν=1/3 Laughlin state on IonQ's trapped-ion quantum computer using a 16-qubit circuit with 369 two-qubit gates and a Hamiltonian variational ansatz — the first demonstration of this topological phase embodying fractionalization, anyonic excitations, and incompressibility on a digital quantum processor (bosonic analogs existed on simulators). With symmetry-verification error mitigation, measured observables including the correlation hole, bulk-edge correspondence, and topological entanglement entropy agreed strongly with exact diagonalization benchmarks.
**Links:**
- [Nature Communications — Realization of fermionic Laughlin state on a quantum processor](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-72769-y)

**Commentary:** Porting condensed-matter topological order onto commercial quantum hardware provides an end-to-end workflow for simulating strongly correlated materials — though scale remains far below classically intractable systems.

### 14. Solid–Solid Catalysis in Hydrogen-Based Alloy Synthesis Opens Greener Fe–Ni Metallurgy Path
**Summary:** Per Nature Synthesis (June 8, 2026; DOI: 10.1038/s44160-026-01086-5), Chen et al. report that during hydrogen co-reduction of Fe₂O₃–NiO mixtures, solid–solid catalytic interaction between pre-reduced Ni metal and transient FeO oxide accelerates hydrogen reduction by at least a factor of two. Ni partitions across metal–oxide interfaces, continuously regenerating catalytic sites that promote H₂ spillover; rapid fcc Fe–Ni alloy forms at 700°C, bypassing conventional bulk-metal nucleation. Global metal production accounts for ~10% of greenhouse gas emissions; the pathway offers kinetic and commercial advantages for hydrogen-based ironmaking and integrated alloy production.
**Links:**
- [Nature Synthesis — Solid–solid catalysis for sustainable alloy synthesis](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44160-026-01086-5)

**Commentary:** Solid-state catalysis pushes "decarbonized steelmaking" from thermodynamic feasibility toward kinetic scalability — but industrial hydrogen supply and feedstock purity remain prerequisites.
