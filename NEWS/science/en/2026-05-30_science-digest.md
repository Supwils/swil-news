### 4. Cincinnati / Johns Hopkins: Electrospun Nanofiber Implant Delivers Three-Drug Combo, More Than Doubles Glioblastoma Mouse Survival
**Summary:** Per Phys.org on May 30, 2026, a University of Cincinnati and Johns Hopkins Medicine team embedded three approved drugs — temozolomide, acriflavine, and HIF-2α inhibitor PT2385 — in an electrospun nanofiber mesh implant, exploiting synergistic effects against glioblastoma. Untreated mice all died within 19 days; most treated mice lived roughly twice as long, and 40% survived past the 120-day endpoint with a plateau lasting more than 80 days. The triple combination showed strong synergy across multiple glioblastoma models and can deliver both immediate and sustained doses. Results appear in ACS Biomaterials Science & Engineering; lead author Daewoo Han.
**Links:**
- [Phys.org — Nanofiber implant delivers three drugs, doubles survival in glioblastoma mice](https://phys.org/news/2026-05-nanofiber-implant-drugs-survival-glioblastoma.html)

**Commentary:** Adult glioblastoma still lacks effective local delivery — an "old drugs, new combo + implantable matrix" path, if validated in larger animals and clinics, could offer a manufacturable option for post-resection cavity therapy.

### 5. Nature Communications: Bispecific T-Cell Engager Teclistamab Shows Rapid Improvement in Two Refractory Autoimmune Neuropathy Patients
**Summary:** Wickel, Ceanga, Vlad, et al. report in Nature Communications (online May 30, 2026) on two chronic immune-mediated peripheral nerve myelinopathy (CIPNM) patients — one IgM-κ–associated, one anti-MAG antibody–mediated — refractory to standard care including rituximab and plasma exchange. After four doses of the approved BCMA×CD3 bispecific T-cell engager teclistamab, walking distance, grip strength, and nerve conduction improved markedly; one patient's walking distance increased more than sixfold over six months, and serum neurofilament light chain fell, indicating reduced axonal injury. IgM paraprotein and MAG antibodies became undetectable about six weeks after the first dose; no serious adverse events occurred, only manageable cytokine release syndrome and hypogammaglobulinemia. The compassionate-use study was conducted at Jena University Hospital, Germany.
**Links:**
- [Nature Communications — Therapeutic effect of T-cell engager in two patients with autoimmune neuropathy](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-73819-1)

**Commentary:** Repurposing an off-the-shelf oncology BiTE for B-cell/plasma-cell–driven neuroimmunology offers a middle option between CAR-T cost and conventional immunotherapy — but with only two patients and up to nine months follow-up, randomized trials remain essential.

## III. Climate, Energy & Environment

### 9. Nature Communications: AMOC Slowdown Amplifies North Atlantic Upper-Ocean Salinity Variability to Unprecedented Levels
**Summary:** Iwakiri, Kug, An, et al. report in Nature Communications (online May 30, 2026) that under sustained global warming, Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC) weakening triggers extreme upper-ocean salinity variability in the North Atlantic with amplitudes far exceeding historical levels. Variability amplifies as it propagates across the basin, linked to Gulf Stream slowdown and strengthened spatial salinity contrasts; an idealized model shows mean-state shifts promote salinity extremes with amplification critically dependent on salinity–temperature coupled feedback. Once AMOC slowdown begins, enhanced variability can persist even under later mitigation because ocean circulation recovers slowly; compound salinity and sea-level extremes raise risks for European coastal ecosystems and societies.
**Links:**
- [Nature Communications — AMOC slowdown amplifies North Atlantic salinity variability to unprecedented levels](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-73838-y)

**Commentary:** Coastal adaptation should treat circulation "mean drift" and "variability amplification" separately — planning on sea-level means alone may underestimate compound shocks to water supply, aquaculture, and infrastructure from salinity pulses.

### 10. Nature Geoscience: ~700 mm Mean Annual Precipitation Marks Ecosystem Nitrogen-Retention Control Shift
**Summary:** Per Phys.org on May 30, 2026, a team led by Prof. Liu Lingli at the Institute of Botany, Chinese Academy of Sciences, published in Nature Geoscience using soil δ15N from 31 U.S. NEON sites as a long-term integrator of nitrogen retention versus loss, identifying a robust mean annual precipitation (MAP) threshold near 700 mm aligned with North America's arid–humid divide (the 100th meridian, shifting toward the 98th under climate change). Below 700 mm, rising MAP lowers δ15N, reflecting stronger retention with plant diversity and microbial competition as key controls; above 700 mm, further wetting increases δ15N via hydrological leaching and denitrification, with soil C/N, nitrate, and clay dominating. Shifting hydroclimatic boundaries could reshape continental nitrogen cycling under precipitation extremes and improve Earth system model parameterization.
**Links:**
- [Phys.org — Rainfall near 700 mm marks turning point in ecosystem nitrogen retention](https://phys.org/news/2026-05-rainfall-mm-ecosystem-nitrogen-retention.html)
- [Nature Geoscience — Precipitation threshold-driven shifts in dominant controls of ecosystem nitrogen retention](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41561-026-01992-5)

**Commentary:** Translating "rain-belt migration" into a switch for nitrogen moving from biotic pools to waterways has direct policy implications for fertilization strategy and eutrophication management.

## IV. Basic Research, Astronomy & Neuroscience

### 12. Chandra Deep Observations of CTA 1 Pulsar Wind Nebula: Low Magnetization, PeV-Scale Particle Acceleration Laboratory
**Summary:** Per Phys.org on May 30, 2026, Seth Gagnon (George Washington University) and colleagues used NASA's Chandra X-ray Observatory for deep observations of the pulsar wind nebula (PWN) inside supernova remnant CTA 1, combined with Fermi gamma-ray archival data; the paper appeared on arXiv May 20 (DOI: 10.48550/arxiv.2605.21278). Analysis reveals hard X-ray spectra and low radiative efficiency in compact PWN components, with broadband modeling indicating low magnetic fields (~1.4–3.2 µG) and high electron cutoff energies (~0.2–0.3 PeV); morphology suggests asymmetric expansion interacting with the remnant. Authors describe CTA 1 as an important low-magnetization laboratory for particle acceleration and evolution in young PWNe.
**Links:**
- [Phys.org — Pulsar wind nebula inside supernova remnant explored with Chandra](https://phys.org/news/2026-05-pulsar-nebula-supernova-remnant-explored.html)

**Commentary:** With PeV cosmic-ray origins still unresolved, nearby young PWNe offer spatially resolved "natural particle accelerators" for direct comparison with theory.

### 13. Nature Communications: 'Rhythmic Architecture' Framework Reveals Two Universal Modes of Neural Dynamics
**Summary:** Karvat, Crespo-García, Vishne, et al. report in Nature Communications (online May 30, 2026) that beyond classical frequency-band oscillations, neural activity organizes along sustained versus bursty rhythmicity; the team developed rhythmicity metrics applied to neurophysiological spectra from 859 subjects aged 18+, finding the architecture generalizes across brain regions, cognitive states, and tasks. The work argues rhythmicity adds a dimension to brain-organization principles with potential impact on neurotechnology and clinical diagnostics.
**Links:**
- [Nature Communications — Universal rhythmic architecture uncovers two modes of neural dynamics](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-026-73553-8)

**Commentary:** Moving EEG/MEG interpretation from "which band is stronger" to "how rhythm is temporally structured" offers new syntax for biomarker stratification in brain–computer interfaces and neuropsychiatric disease.
