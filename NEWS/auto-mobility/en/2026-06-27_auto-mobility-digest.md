### 1. USMCA joint review starts in four days; U.S. pushes auto RVC to 82% and 50% U.S. content (Policy / North America)
**Summary:** Mexico Business News, AS/COA, and Carra Globe reported in June 2026 that the first USMCA joint review formally begins on July 1, 2026, with Mexico's Economy Minister Ebrard confirming text-based talks in Mexico City on July 20. In bilateral consultations during May–June, Washington proposed sharply tightening automotive rules of origin: raising Regional Value Content (RVC) from the current 75% to 82% and adding a 50% U.S.-specific content requirement, while pushing to restrict Chinese-origin components in engines, batteries, and other core parts and to replace Labor Value Content (LVC) self-certification with third-party audits. Mexico's second negotiation round on June 15–17 covered autos, steel and aluminum, and economic security, but a comprehensive deal by July 1 is considered unlikely, leaving scenarios such as a "painful extension" or annual reviews on the table. Mexican auto exports to the U.S. fell 5.1% year over year to $48.638 billion from January through April 2025, with Section 232 tariffs continuing to pressure supply chains.

## II. Autonomous Driving & Safety

### 2. Tesla settles 2023 FSD pedestrian-death lawsuit; NHTSA engineering analysis of 3.2M vehicles continues (Autonomous Driving / U.S.)
**Summary:** Electrek, TechCrunch, and Jalopnik reported on June 26, 2026, that Tesla reached a settlement with the family of Johna Story, killed on November 28, 2023, in an Arizona crash involving a Model Y operating in Full Self-Driving (FSD) mode; terms were not disclosed. Story, 71, had stepped out of her vehicle to help direct traffic around an earlier glare-related pileup when she was struck at high speed — the first known pedestrian fatality linked to FSD. The National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) opened a preliminary evaluation in October 2024 and upgraded it in March 2026 to an Engineering Analysis (EA26002) covering an estimated 3.2 million Tesla vehicles, examining whether FSD can detect degraded performance in glare, dust, and other low-visibility conditions and warn drivers in time; NHTSA documents show Tesla did not begin developing a related detection update until June 28, 2024 — the day after it filed the required crash report. The settlement does not close the federal probe; a mandatory recall remains possible.

- [Electrek — Tesla settles lawsuit over fatal 'Full Self-Driving' pedestrian crash](https://electrek.co/2026/06/26/tesla-fsd-pedestrian-death-settlement/)
- [TechCrunch — Tesla settles FSD crash lawsuit as federal investigations continue](https://techcrunch.com/2026/06/26/tesla-settles-fsd-crash-lawsuit-as-federal-investigations-continue/)

### 3. Waymo recalls 3,871 5th-gen ADS units after robotaxis entered closed freeway construction zones (Autonomous Driving / U.S.)
**Summary:** NHTSA recall file 26E035 and TechCrunch reported in June 2026 that Waymo filed a voluntary software recall covering 3,871 fifth-generation Automated Driving System (ADS) units. In April–May 2026, at least 13 incidents occurred in Phoenix and the San Francisco Bay Area in which robotaxis entered closed freeway construction zones at speed; software either prioritized avoiding other freeway hazards or failed to recognize the work zones. Waymo's Field Safety Committee restricted all freeway operations from May 19, and its Safety Board decided on June 8 to conduct a recall; the remedy is an over-the-air update to improve construction-zone detection and avoidance, still under development. Waymo reported no related injuries; city-street service is unaffected.

**Links:**
