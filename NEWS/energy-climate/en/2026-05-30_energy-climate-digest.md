## I. Policy & Carbon Markets

### 1. California CARB Overhauls Cap-and-Invest Carbon Market Rules (Policy)
**Summary:** The California Air Resources Board (CARB) voted 9–4 on May 29 to adopt sweeping revisions to the state's 13-year-old carbon market (Cap-and-Invest, formerly cap-and-trade). The update will grant roughly $3.5–4 billion worth of free allowances to oil refiners and manufacturers if they build emissions-reduction projects; it also adds $2 billion from 2027 through 2030 for utility bill credits to residents and sets aside about $800 million to help businesses limit pass-through costs. The nonpartisan Legislative Analyst's Office estimates annual revenues for the Greenhouse Gas Reduction Fund could be cut in half — the fund previously received about $4 billion per year for climate mitigation, affordable housing, and transit. Environmental groups and some Democratic lawmakers strongly opposed the move; the oil industry said it still fails to address energy affordability.

## II. Renewables & Storage

### 3. India Plans ₹15,000 Crore VGF Scheme for 112 GWh of Storage (Storage)
**Summary:** According to The Economic Times on May 30, the Indian government is drafting a new Viability Gap Funding (VGF) scheme with an outlay of roughly ₹15,000 crore (~$1.8 billion) to support 112 gigawatt-hours (GWh) of storage capacity — 50 GWh of battery energy storage (BESS), 60 GWh of pumped storage, and 2 GWh of emerging technologies. A draft has been circulated for inter-ministerial consultation. The plan targets record summer electricity demand and evening peak loads while supporting the 2030 goal of 500 GW non-fossil capacity. The Central Electricity Authority projects storage needs of 235 GWh by 2029–30 and 888 GWh by 2035–36; earlier BESS VGF tranches supported 13.2 GWh (30% capex subsidy) and 30 GWh (16%) but excluded pumped hydro.

### 4. Kyushu University Team Reports 130% Quantum Yield — Two Excitons from One Photon (Renewables)
**Summary:** Researchers at Kyushu University and collaborators in Germany reported in the Journal of the American Chemical Society (JACS) on May 29 a photovoltaic system pairing a molybdenum-based "spin-flip" complex with a singlet-fission material, achieving approximately 130% quantum yield — meaning an average of 1.3 usable excitons harvested per absorbed photon. Singlet fission can split one high-energy blue-light photon into two triplet excitons; traditionally many are lost via Förster resonance energy transfer. The spin-flip emitter selectively captures triplet excitons, bypassing that loss channel. Authors stress this does not violate energy conservation but recovers excitons previously written off; singlet-fission cells could theoretically reach 35–45% efficiency if translated to devices.

**Links:**

- [Energies Media — Kyushu University researchers achieved a 130% quantum yield in solar energy conversion](https://energiesmedia.com/kyushu-university-130-percent-quantum-yield-solar-energy/)

**Commentary:** Lab numbers remain far from mass production, but the molecular pathway for recovering lost excitons offers a verified mechanism to push beyond silicon's Shockley-Queisser limit.

## IV. Oil, Gas & Energy Security

### 8. IEA: "Largest Ever" Energy Security Crisis; Global Energy Investment to Hit $3.4 Trillion in 2026 (Oil & Gas)
**Summary:** The IEA released its World Energy Investment 2026 report on May 28, with Executive Director Fatih Birol warning the Middle East conflict is triggering "the largest energy security crisis the world has ever faced," reshaping investment patterns akin to the 1970s oil shocks. IEA expects global energy investment of roughly $3.4 trillion in 2026 — about $2.2 trillion toward grids, storage, low-emission fuels, renewables, nuclear, and efficiency, and $1.2 trillion toward oil, gas, and coal. Gas investment is projected to rise to $330 billion, the highest in a decade; grid spending ~$550 billion; battery storage over $100 billion; upstream oil investment falls for a third straight year below $500 billion. Some Asian countries are extending coal plant lifetimes amid supply fears; coal investment may reach $180 billion in 2026 (a decade high), with China accounting for ~70% of global coal supply spending.
