## I. Policy & Carbon Markets

### 1. France signs national electrification pact, targeting 60% domestically produced electricity by 2030 (Policy)
**Summary:** CleanTechnica and RFI reported on June 7 and May 28 that President Emmanuel Macron signed a national electrification pact with roughly 200 companies at the Élysée, involving some 6,000 firms and claiming more than 600,000 jobs created or maintained. The government plans to double annual public electrification support from about €5.5 billion to €10 billion per year through 2030, covering generation, heating, transport, and industry. Measures include one million heat pumps per year by 2030, a ban on gas heating in new buildings, restarted social EV leasing for lower-income high-mileage drivers, expanded charging networks, and 45,000 km of new power lines. The plan responds directly to fossil fuel import shocks triggered by the Middle East conflict — fossil fuels currently account for about 58% of France's final energy consumption, with PPE3 targeting a reduction to 40% by 2030.

**Links:**

- [CleanTechnica — France Gets Electrification Right, But 2030 Is Doing A Lot Of Work](https://cleantechnica.com/2026/06/07/france-electrification-plan-energy-security/)
- [RFI — France bets on 'desirable' electric future, thanks to major private funding](https://www.rfi.fr/en/france/20260528-france-bets-on-desirable-electric-future-thanks-to-major-private-funding)

**Commentary:** France is elevating electrification from a climate side dish to an energy sovereignty main course — directionally correct, but the 2030 deadline will only hold if heat pump installers, transformers, and grid connections keep pace with politics.

### 2. China achieves first 50% green hydrogen co-firing and 100% pure hydrogen combustion in coal boilers (Policy)
**Summary:** China Economic Net and CCTV reported on June 7 that China Energy Investment Corporation's self-developed hydrogen-coal co-combustion low-NOx burner completed a major trial: 50% heat-ratio green hydrogen co-firing and 100% pure hydrogen combustion on a test rig, with a full safety system from hydrogen delivery to furnace combustion. With green hydrogen, coal savings and carbon reductions can reach 50%, while NOx emissions are effectively controlled. Given China's world-largest coal power fleet, the technology validates a potential path for large-scale carbon reduction at coal units and has practical significance for green transition and coal-renewable integration.

**Links:**

- [China Economic Net — Major breakthrough in China's clean low-carbon hydrogen-coal co-firing technology](http://www.ce.cn/xwzx/gnsz/gdxw/202606/t20260607_3015252.shtml)

**Commentary:** In a coal-heavy system, "co-fire rather than retire" is more transition-realistic than abrupt shutdowns — the question is whether green hydrogen cost and supply can support scale-up.

### 3. IATA warns SAF prices hit $3,000/tonne as production stagnates since December 2025 (Carbon market)
**Summary:** The Straits Times reported on June 7 that IATA, at its June 6 sustainability briefing during the annual general meeting in Rio de Janeiro, said sustainable aviation fuel (SAF) prices rose from just over $2,000/tonne in February before the Middle East conflict to about $3,000/tonne in May. Production has stagnated since December 2025; 2026 output is expected at only 2.4 million tonnes — less than 1% of global jet fuel demand — despite global SAF capacity expanding to 9 million tonnes/year. IATA proposes a "book and claim" system to decouple physical delivery from emission reductions, and on June 6 launched an alliance to boost CORSIA Eligible Emissions Units (EEUs), targeting 175 million EEUs brought to market by end-2026 for a total of 213 million.

**Links:**

- [The Straits Times — IATA urges bigger green aviation fuel push as production stagnates and prices rise](https://www.straitstimes.com/singapore/iata-urges-bigger-green-aviation-fuel-push-as-production-stagnates-and-prices-rise)

**Commentary:** Aviation's biggest decarbonization lever is stuck on feedstock and policy tilt — idle SAF capacity with surging prices means "no feedstock" beats "no technology" as the binding constraint.

## II. Clean Power & Storage

### 5. China's solar majors pivot to storage exports; 2026 battery exports may rise 30% to 150 GWh (Clean power)
**Summary:** Reuters reported on June 5 that as domestic PV sales slow and export growth hits its lowest since 2018 (panel exports up only 4.7% in 2025), leaders including JinkoSolar, LONGi, Trina, and JA Solar are pushing solar-plus-storage integration at SNEC. Jinko plans to nearly triple battery capacity from 5 GWh to 13–14 GWh; Rystad Energy expects China's energy storage battery exports to rise 30% to 150 GWh in 2026. CATL expects storage to reach 50% of its global sales by 2030, up from about 25% now. Manufacturers are leveraging supply chain strengths to enter a market dominated by CATL and BYD, using integrated systems to offset module price wars.

**Links:**

- [Reuters — China's solar majors charge into batteries as panel sales falter](https://www.investing.com/news/economic-indicators/chinas-solar-majors-charge-into-batteries-as-panel-sales-falter-4727761)

**Commentary:** PV overcapacity is spilling into storage — the same players are replacing "sell panels" with "sell systems," and the next battlefield for global renewables is batteries, not modules.

### 6. Malaysia mandates BESS for all LSS6 solar tenders, targeting ~2,000 MW phased deployment (Storage)
**Summary:** New Straits Times reported on June 3 that Deputy Prime Minister and Minister of Energy Transition and Water Transformation Fadillah Yusof announced all new solar projects under the sixth Large Scale Solar (LSS6) program must include battery energy storage systems (BESS) to strengthen grid stability alongside renewable expansion. Malaysia targets roughly 2,000 MW of BESS capacity in phases, adjustable to demand; 100 MW is already commissioned and another 400 MW is under development. Power supply-demand projections are reviewed every six months, with acceleration possible if demand exceeds expectations.

### 8. Chile's Colbún 228 MW/912 MWh BESS becomes world's first storage project authorized to sell Article 6.2 carbon credits (Carbon market)
**Summary:** BNamericas reported April 7 and in subsequent interviews that Colbún's 228 MW/912 MWh lithium-ion BESS at Diego de Almagro Sur in Chile's Atacama region, in cooperation with Switzerland's KliK Foundation, became the world's first storage project authorized to sell carbon credits under Paris Agreement Article 6.2 for use in national NDCs. Adjacent to the Diego de Almagro Sur solar park, the project captures daytime solar surpluses, reduces curtailment, and discharges during nighttime peaks to displace fossil generation. Chile's carbon pricing roadmap prioritizes advanced BESS as high-mitigation technology, with a first-cycle cap of 10 million ITMOs for international transfer — early movers get the quota.

**Links:**

- [BNamericas — Colbún Battery Project is the first in the world authorized to sell carbon credits under the Paris Agreement](https://www.bnamericas.com/en/news/colbun-battery-project-is-the-first-in-the-world-authorized-to-sell-carbon-credits-under-the-paris-agreement)

**Commentary:** Storage is upgrading from "grid accessory" to "carbon asset" — Article 6 opens a new financing channel for curtailment-heavy Latin American markets, but the additionality window won't stay open forever.

### 11. Senegal mangrove restoration exposed as "ghost carbon," storage possibly overstated by ~168,000 tonnes CO₂ (Carbon market)
**Summary:** Recent research in The Conversation found that in Senegal's 10,000-hectare mangrove restoration project, more than one-third of plots completely failed with no surviving mangroves. When Verra approved carbon credits in 2020, monitor Agresta extrapolated from sample tree measurements across the full project, estimating only 18% failure; satellite analysis shows far lower actual carbon storage — about 76,000 tonnes CO₂ in trees and soil combined, a gap of roughly 168,000 tonnes CO₂ versus certification, potentially worth $2–7 million at current prices. Authors urge certifiers and monitors to assess failure rates, or they are selling "ghost carbon" with no benefit to people or the environment.

**Links:**

- [The Conversation — How did a major mangrove restoration project in Senegal end up selling 'ghost carbon'?](https://theconversation.com/how-did-a-major-mangrove-restoration-project-in-senegal-end-up-selling-ghost-carbon-283380)

**Commentary:** Quality gaps in the blue carbon rush — MRV without failure-rate auditing turns voluntary carbon markets into accounting games.
