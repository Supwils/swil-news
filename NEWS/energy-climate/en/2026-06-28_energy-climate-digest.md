### 1. Australian Federal Court Accepts UN Special Rapporteur in North West Shelf Gas Extension Challenge (Policy · Law)
**Summary:** The Australian Conservation Foundation (ACF) reported on **June 28, 2026** that the Federal Court has accepted UN Special Rapporteur on the Human Right to a Clean, Healthy and Sustainable Environment Astrid Puentes Riaño as amicus curiae in legal challenges to Woodside's North West Shelf LNG hub extension to **2070**. ACF is suing Environment Minister Murray Watt for failing to assess climate pollution harms from the project. The rapporteur will address Australia's international law obligations and how that context should inform interpretation of "impact" under national environment law (EPBC Act). A **July 2025** International Court of Justice advisory opinion held that fossil fuel exporters cannot export responsibility for climate impacts of fuels they sell. ACF research shows lifetime emissions from the project would be nearly **10 times** Australia's annual emissions. Hearings are set for **July** in Melbourne; Woodside previously opposed the intervention on procedural grounds.

### 2. EU Energy Ministers Win Greater Role in Cross-Border Grid Planning Under Grids Package Compromise (Policy)
**Summary:** Daily Guardian Europe reported on **June 26, 2026** that EU energy ministers in Luxembourg reached a compromise on the Grids Package after political rows with the European Commission over who controls Europe's future electricity system. Key issues included a new centralized planning scenario for grid investments and sweeping measures to accelerate clean-power permitting, which often takes **5–10 years**. Under pressure from Sweden, France and others, the Cypriot presidency softened the Commission's **December 2025** proposal: national authorities will verify data and assumptions in the central scenario, and planners must account for national and regional circumstances and latest national climate plans. Measures include digital one-stop permitting portals, renewable projects presumed in the overriding public interest, tacit approval options and simplified procedures. The European Parliament industry committee votes on **July 1**; analysts say Europe's bottleneck has shifted from generation expansion to insufficient transmission capacity.

**Links:**

- [Daily Guardian Europe — EU energy ministers secure greater role in bloc's future power grid after rows with Brussels](https://dailyguardian.eu/eu-energy-ministers-secure-greater-role-in-blocs-future-power-grid-after-rows-with-brussels/)

**Commentary:** Ministers in Luxembourg fought over who draws the map—renewable capacity is largely planned; what's missing are lines and permits to move power from the North Sea to factories.

### 3. Paris Court Orders TotalEnergies to Disclose Scope 3 Emissions and Strengthen Climate Strategy (Carbon Markets · Law)
**Summary:** European Pulse reported on **June 26, 2026** that the Paris judicial court ruled Tuesday that TotalEnergies must publicly account for indirect greenhouse gas emissions (scope 3) and revise its climate transition plan to better align with Paris Agreement goals. The case was brought by NGOs including Notre Affaire à Tous and Greenpeace France—one of Europe's most ambitious climate challenges to a major oil company. The court did not hold TotalEnergies directly liable for customer combustion emissions but ordered a full value-chain assessment and stricter climate policy; the ruling applies to French and global operations, reflecting extraterritorial reach of French corporate law. The decision goes beyond EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) disclosure by mandating strategy adjustment, not just reporting.

**Links:**

- [European Pulse — Paris Court Orders TotalEnergies to Disclose Indirect Emissions and Strengthen Climate Strategy](https://europeanpulse.com/paris-court-orders-totalenergies-to-disclose-indirect-emissions-and-strengthen-climate-strategy/)

**Commentary:** Scope 3 is upgrading from disclosure duty to strategy duty—European courts are pushing oil sellers' climate ledgers from investor reports into operating decisions.

**Summary:** Anadolu Agency reported on **June 28, 2026** that WHO Director-General Tedros Adhanom Ghebreyesus said on social media that more than **1,300** excess deaths linked to high temperatures have been recorded across Europe since **June 21**. ### 8. World's First **16 MW** Tension-Leg Floating Offshore Wind Platform Sets Sail in China, **54 GWh** Annual Output (Clean Power)
**Summary:** Global Times (via China Media Group) reported on **June 28, 2026** that the world's first **16 MW** tension-leg platform (TLP) floating offshore wind unit departed Gaolan Port, Zhuhai, Guangdong, marking accelerated commercialization of China's deep-sea floating wind. Standing about **307 meters** tall and weighing nearly **8,000 tonnes**, it is expected to generate roughly **54 million kWh** annually, cutting CO₂ by about **35,000 tonnes** and fuel oil by roughly **15,000 cubic meters** per year. Power will be transmitted via subsea cables directly to an offshore oilfield grid, exploring a low-carbon model combining deep-sea wind with oil and gas development; industry observers say it will upgrade the Pearl River Delta floating-wind supply chain. China has made major offshore wind infrastructure progress this year, including floatover installation of the world's largest offshore converter station "Heart of Sea Wind" in early **June**.

**Commentary:** Floating wind is moving beyond demonstration—feeding offshore oilfield grids sells clean power to the hardest-to-abate offshore customer and anchors a deepwater equipment supply chain.

### 9. India Commissions World's First Nuclear Heat-Driven Hydrogen Production Plant at Kalpakkam (Green Hydrogen)
**Summary:** Republic World and official releases reported **June 26–28, 2026** that India commissioned the world's first hydrogen facility using nuclear reactor process heat at the Indira Gandhi Centre for Atomic Research (IGCAR), Kalpakkam, Tamil Nadu, inaugurated by Atomic Energy Commission Chairman Ajit Kumar Mohanty on **June 26**. The plant uses an indigenous copper-chlorine (Cu–Cl) thermochemical cycle powered by the Fast Breeder Test Reactor (FBTR), enabling continuous, energy-efficient hydrogen production without grid electricity and avoiding renewable intermittency. Officials target hard-to-abate sectors including steel, fertilizer and refining, extending India's three-stage nuclear program into clean hydrogen and paving the way for advanced reactors to co-produce electricity and carbon-free hydrogen.

**Commentary:** Nuclear heat hydrogen welds baseload clean power to molecular fuels—if scaled, the green hydrogen debate shifts from "is there renewable electricity?" to "is there stable high-temperature heat?"

### 10. France Proposes State Compensation for Offshore Wind "Wake Theft," North Sea Nations May Follow (Policy · Wind)
**Summary:** Energies Media reported on **June 28, 2026** that France has proposed a mechanism whereby the state would compensate offshore wind operators whose output is measurably reduced by neighboring projects, rather than leaving developers to litigate among themselves. The plan does not require upstream developers to admit fault or pay directly; it treats wake-effect losses as a systemic consequence of dense development policy and assigns the state a compensating role. Industry observers say if workable, the model could be a template for the Netherlands, Germany, Denmark and the UK. As France accelerates offshore deployment, wake conflicts are upgrading from technical disputes to institutional design problems.

**Links:**

- [Energies Media — France proposed paying offshore wind farms robbed of wind by newer neighbors](https://energiesmedia.com/france-offshore-wind-farm-compensation/)

**Commentary:** "Stolen wind" is moving from courtrooms to finance ministries—as offshore density rises, governments must intervene or adjacent projects fall into zero-sum conflict that slows overall buildout.
