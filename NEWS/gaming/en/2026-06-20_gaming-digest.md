## I. Regulation & Policy

### 1. EU Declines "Stop Killing Games" Legislation, Pivots to Voluntary Industry Code

**Summary:** On June 20, The Register and other outlets reported that the European Commission has formally declined to propose new legislation requiring publishers to keep video games playable after commercial support ends, in response to the European Citizens' Initiative (ECI) "Stop Destroying Videogames," which gathered over 1.29 million verified signatures. In its June 16 response, the Commission cited existing copyright and intellectual property frameworks as barriers to imposing such obligations, while noting that current EU consumer law already requires providers to disclose service duration and termination terms upfront and offers proportional refunds when content does not conform. Instead, the Commission plans stakeholder dialogue by end-2026 toward a voluntary industry code of conduct on game end-of-life management, plus a report on the application of the digital content directive. Campaign founder Ross Scott called the outcome "not unexpected" and is now pushing to attach related amendments to the upcoming Digital Fairness Act, expected as a formal proposal in Q4 2026.

- [European Commission — Commission will engage with industry following ECI on disabling videogames (Jun 16)](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/news/commission-will-engage-industry-following-european-citizens-initiative-disabling-videogames)

**Commentary:** 1.3 million signatures could not force a legal obligation — but they anchored digital ownership onto the agenda of the next consumer-law package.

### 2. Nine EU Regulators Coordinate World Cup Crackdown on Unlicensed Prediction Markets

**Summary:** On June 19, gambling regulators from Belgium, France, Germany, Italy, the Netherlands, Poland, Portugal, Spain, and Switzerland issued a joint statement timed to the start of the 2026 FIFA World Cup, pledging cross-border cooperation against unlicensed prediction market platforms. Measures may include service blocking, fines, advertising restrictions, and account freezes, with particular focus on operators relying on offshore or decentralized crypto licenses. Spain's DGOJ had already ordered temporary blocks on Polymarket and Kalshi; France and the Netherlands have run similar geoblocking efforts. The nine bodies also plan increased safer-gambling social media campaigns during the tournament.
