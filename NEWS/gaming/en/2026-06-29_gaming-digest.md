## III. Regulation & Compliance

### 5. South Korea Cracks Down on Unregistered "Gametel" PC Gaming in Lodging Rooms

**Summary:** On June 28 (Sunday), South Korea's Ministry of Culture, Sports and Tourism and the Game Rating and Administration Committee announced a nationwide administrative guidance campaign against illegal unregistered internet PC game operations in lodging facilities—so-called "gametels," where motels and similar venues provide in-room PCs with games without proper registration. The ministry sent official letters to metropolitan and provincial governments seeking cooperation and asked three major domestic lodging reservation platforms to voluntarily manage online and on-site ads using phrases such as "game room" and "PC cafe." The committee also met with PC cafe industry groups. After a prior guidance period, joint crackdowns with local governments and the National Police Agency are planned against businesses with confirmed illegal activity. Current law prohibits lodging operators from installing more than two game-equipped PCs per room; violations can carry up to two years imprisonment or a 20 million won fine, though enforcement has been limited for years.

### 6. Brazilian Court Orders ~$58.7M in Loot Box Damages from Major Game Companies

**Summary:** Judge Rejane Zenir Jungbluth of Brazil's 1st Child and Youth Court of the Federal District ordered Apple, Microsoft, Tencent, Google, Sony, EA, Riot, Garena, Ubisoft, Valve, Konami, and Nintendo to pay a combined ~BRL 298 million (~$58.7 million USD) in collective moral damages over loot box mechanics, in a suit filed by the National Association of Centers for the Defense of Children and Adolescents (Anced). Riot was fined ~$2.9 million; Valve ~$1.96 million. The ruling is subject to appeal. If upheld, companies must within 90 days add explicit warnings, disclose probabilities, implement age verification, and establish refund mechanisms, or face daily fines up to BRL 100,000. The decision cites Brazil's 1990 Child and Adolescent Statute and the Digital ECA (Law 15,211/25) enacted March 2026—distinct from Riot's 2026 age-rating upgrades for League of Legends and related titles, as this case dates to 2021.
