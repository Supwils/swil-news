## I. Major Studios & Strategy

### 1. Xbox Confirms Ninja Theory Closure; Double Fine and Compulsion in Spinoff Talks

**Summary:** From the evening of June 15 into June 16, The Verge and Bloomberg reported that Microsoft’s Xbox division is shutting down Hellblade developer Ninja Theory. Cambridge staff were told on a Monday call that the studio is closing, though the team is seeking a buyer to keep operations alive. The news came just nine days after Xbox Games Showcase unveiled Senua, the third Hellblade entry targeting a 2027 release — its future is now unclear. Psychonauts maker Double Fine and South of Midnight developer Compulsion Games are in "active negotiations" to spin off and regain independence, similar to Toys for Bob leaving Activision; even if deals succeed, large layoffs are expected. The moves are part of CEO Asha Sharma’s 100-day "business reset" announced last week, with broader layoffs and budget cuts expected after the fiscal year ends on June 30.

### 3. Fox–Roku $22B Deal Continues to Draw Market Scrutiny

**Summary:** On June 16, industry and market analysis continued around Fox Corporation’s acquisition of streaming pioneer Roku, announced the prior day. Fox will pay $160 per Roku share ($96 cash plus 0.9693 Fox Class A shares), valuing the deal at roughly $22 billion including debt; closing is expected in H1 2027, pending shareholder and regulatory approval. The merger combines Fox sports, news, and entertainment with Tubi, Roku’s 100M+ global households, The Roku Channel, and first-party data. By Nielsen viewing share, the combined entity would rank third in U.S. television behind YouTube and Disney. Roku pledged to remain an open platform; The Roku Channel and Tubi will operate as separate brands. Fox shares fell roughly 11% in pre-market trading.

## II. Regulation & Policy

### 4. Netherlands Confirms Five-Year Online Gambling Crackdown: Ad Ban, Bonus Ban, Deposit Caps

**Summary:** On June 16, Gaming Intelligence reported that the Dutch Secretary of State for Justice and Security has confirmed the coalition gambling policy adopted in February, targeting a "structural reduction in gambling harm" by 2030. Core measures include a total ban on online gambling advertising, prohibition of sign-up bonuses, cross-platform deposit limits, affordability checks, and improvements to the Cruks self-exclusion system. A planned minimum-age increase from 18 to 21 for online casino play was dropped over fears of pushing young players to illegal sites. Trade body VNLOK warns ad and bonus bans may drive traffic to black markets; parliament will debate the package on June 24.

**Links:**

- [Gaming Intelligence — Netherlands confirms new online gambling policy (Jun 16)](https://www.gamingintelligence.com/legal/232059-netherlands-government-confirms-new-online-gambling-policy/)

**Commentary:** Europe keeps rediscovering that banning ads does not ban gambling — deposit caps and affordability checks are the harder levers.

### 5. Poland Criminalizes Promotion of Illegal Gambling in Streams

**Summary:** On June 16, SBC News reported that Poland’s Sejm passed the "End of Patostreaming Bill" by 419 votes to 19, making influencers and streamers criminally liable for profiting from promoting unlicensed gambling platforms. Police and prosecutors will lead enforcement, with the National Revenue Administration (KAS) involved in gambling investigations. Poland’s online casino market remains a state monopoly under Totalizator Sportowy, with no liberalization in sight; the bill signals Warsaw prioritizes enforcement and youth protection over market expansion.

**Links:**

- [SBC News — Poland criminalizes illegal gambling stream promotion (Jun 16)](https://sbcnews.co.uk/europe/2026/06/16/poland-gambling-streams-legislature/)

**Commentary:** When gambling streams cross from platform compliance into criminal law, Central European influencer monetization gets a new boundary.
