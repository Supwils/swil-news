## I. Regulation & Legislation

### 1. California Assembly Passes Protect Our Games Act — A U.S. Milestone for Stop Killing Games

**Summary:** PC Gamer and IGN reported on May 31 that the California State Assembly passed AB 1921 (Protect Our Games Act) by a vote of 43–16. The bill would require publishers selling paid digital games in California that rely on developer-controlled servers—first offered on or after January 1, 2027—to give at least 60 days' notice before shutting down services necessary for ordinary use, and on shutdown day to provide an offline-capable version, a standalone patch, or a full refund. Free-to-play, subscription, and titles with permanent offline downloads are exempt. Assembly Member Chris Ward announced the result on the Stop Killing Games YouTube channel; the Entertainment Software Association (ESA) opposes the measure, arguing it would divert resources from new development. The bill now moves to the California State Senate, with committee debate expected in June.

### 2. Philippines PAGCOR: May 31 B2B Accreditation Deadline; Full Compliance Due by July 31

**Summary:** AGB reported on May 31 that the Philippine Amusement and Gaming Corporation (PAGCOR) board approved revised e-gaming rules on May 21 and issued a memorandum on May 26: B2B suppliers already serving Gaming System Administrators (GSAs) must submit accreditation applications by May 31 to enter a transition window through July 31; those who miss the deadline may no longer serve any GSA. Full compliance—including fees, probity checks, on-site inspection, and platform testing—is required by July 31, or electronic gaming systems, online platforms, and equipment will be decommissioned from August 1. Revisions also streamline accreditation for banks and payment gateways; the new rules take effect June 4.

### 4. Cortical Labs Trains ~200,000 Living Neurons on a CL1 Chip to Play Doom

**Summary:** Phys.org reported on May 31 that Australian biotech Cortical Labs demonstrated its CL1 "biological computer": roughly 200,000 human neurons grown from stem cells on a silicon microelectrode array, interfaced via a Python API that maps Doom's 3D environment into electrical stimulation and decodes neural spikes into movement and shooting. The team says the neurons show "adaptive, real-time, goal-directed learning," but play remains beginner-level with frequent deaths. Scientific American notes the demo advances biological computing beyond the earlier Pong proof-of-concept toward low-power computing and neurological drug screening.
