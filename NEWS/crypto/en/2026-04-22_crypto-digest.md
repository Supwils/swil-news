### 3. SEC-CFTC Joint Digital Asset Classification: 5 Categories Including Investment Contract Separation
**Summary:** The SEC and CFTC published a joint framework document around April 22, 2026 proposing a **5-category digital asset classification** system — distinguishing between (1) digital commodities, (2) payment stablecoins, (3) digital asset securities, (4) investment contract digital assets, and (5) hybrid assets. The classification explicitly separated "investment contract" characterization from permanent security status, addressing a long-standing industry complaint that the Howey test created perpetual securities classification for assets that evolved toward commodity-like use. The framework was positioned as a precursor to comprehensive legislation.
**Links:**
- [SEC — SEC-CFTC joint digital asset classification framework (2026-04-22)](https://www.sec.gov/news/press-release/2026/digital-asset-classification-framework)
- [Bloomberg Law — What the SEC-CFTC 5-category crypto classification means for markets (2026-04-22)](https://news.bloomberglaw.com/sec-cftc-crypto-classification-2026)
**Commentary:** The investment contract separation is the most consequential element — if finalized, it creates an off-ramp from permanent securities status for projects that can demonstrate commodity-like adoption, which changes the tokenomics calculus for protocol designers.

### 5. China Anti-Foreign Extraterritorial Jurisdiction Regulations: April 13 Effective Date Creates Crypto Compliance Layer
**Summary:** China's **Anti-Foreign Extraterritorial Jurisdiction Regulations**, which took effect on **April 13, 2026**, created new compliance considerations for crypto exchanges and Web3 companies operating in or with China. The regulations impose reporting and approval requirements on Chinese entities (and potentially entities with Chinese personnel or operations) that respond to foreign regulatory orders, subpoenas, or sanctions affecting Chinese persons or assets. Legal commentators noted that for crypto firms, this creates potential conflicts between compliance with U.S. or EU requirements and Chinese regulatory demands.
**Links:**
