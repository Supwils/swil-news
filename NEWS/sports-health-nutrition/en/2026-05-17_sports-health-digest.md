### 1. ACSM releases a landmark 2026 resistance-training Position Stand: bigger evidence base, “consistency over complexity”
**Summary:** The American College of Sports Medicine (ACSM) published an updated Position Stand in *Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise* summarizing resistance-training prescriptions for healthy adults—described in accompanying materials as the first major update on this topic since 2009. ACSM’s announcement says the work synthesized **137 systematic reviews** representing **more than 30,000 participants**, emphasizing that the largest gains often come from moving from little/no resistance training to regular participation. The guidance also discusses tailoring load and volume toward goals such as strength, hypertrophy, and power, and highlights that non-gym modalities (bands, bodyweight) can be effective.

### 3. Cardiac rehabilitation evidence: network meta-analysis highlights HIIT for endothelial function—within a supervised-care frame
**Summary:** *News-Medical* summarizes a systematic review and network meta-analysis covering **37 studies** and **6,818 adults** with coronary artery disease or chronic heart failure, comparing modalities such as moderate continuous aerobic training, high-intensity interval exercise (HIIT), resistance training, and combined programs. The piece reports that HIIT was particularly consistent for improving flow-mediated dilation (FMD), while also noting clinical caveats around individualized prescription and supervision.
**Links:**
- [News-Medical.net — High-intensity interval exercise is most effective for heart patients](https://www.news-medical.net/news/20260504/High-intensity-interval-exercise-is-most-effective-for-heart-patients.aspx)
- [European Journal of Preventive Cardiology — advance article (OUP)](https://academic.oup.com/eurjpc/advance-article/doi/10.1093/eurjpc/zwag118/8490220)
**Commentary:** HIIT can be powerful medicine for some cardiac patients, but intensity without appropriate screening and monitoring is where benefit turns into hazard.

## 2) Health & Medicine

### 5. WHO determines a PHEIC for Bundibugyo Ebola disease in the Democratic Republic of the Congo and Uganda
**Summary:** The WHO Director-General determined a **Public Health Emergency of International Concern (PHEIC)**—while explicitly stating it does **not** meet the IHR criteria for a **pandemic emergency**—for Ebola disease caused by **Bundibugyo virus** in the DRC and Uganda. WHO’s **17 May 2026** statement includes situational data reported **as of 16 May 2026**: **8 laboratory-confirmed cases**, **246 suspected cases**, and **80 suspected deaths** across at least three health zones in Ituri Province, DRC; plus **two laboratory-confirmed cases (including one death)** in Kampala, Uganda linked to travel from the DRC. WHO also stresses major uncertainties in true caseload/spread and notes there are **no approved Bundibugyo-specific therapeutics or vaccines** at present.

## 3) Nutrition

### 7. Four-week dietary shifts and biological-age-related profiles in older adults (early signal, not final proof)
**Summary:** *ScienceDaily* describes University of Sydney work published in *Aging Cell* in which **104 adults aged 65–75** followed four diet patterns for **four weeks**; the story reports improvements in estimated biological age markers in several arms, with the strongest statistical signal described for a lower-fat, higher-carbohydrate omnivorous pattern. Authors caution these are early indicators and that longer trials are needed to link biomarker shifts to hard outcomes.
**Links:**
- [ScienceDaily — 4-week diet change and biological age markers (12 May 2026)](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/05/260511213144.htm)
**Commentary:** This is a useful cautionary tale for headlines: short-term biomarker movement is not the same as proven longevity extension.

### 8. Plant-based dietary patterns and CRP: Warwick review of seven RCTs (n=541)
**Summary:** *Nutrition Insight* summarizes a Warwick-led review in *Nutrition, Metabolism and Cardiovascular Diseases* selecting **seven RCTs** (**541 participants**) and reporting an average C-reactive protein (CRP) reduction of **1.13 mg/L** with plant-based patterns versus omnivorous diets. The article notes effects may be larger when combined with structured exercise, while also flagging the need for larger trials.
**Links:**
- [Nutrition Insight — plant-based diets and inflammation](https://www.nutritioninsight.com/news/plant-based-lowers-inflammation.html)
**Commentary:** For active people, the practical takeaway is that diet quality and training load both modulate systemic inflammation—complementary levers, not competing ideologies.

### 10. Irregular bedtimes in midlife and incident cardiovascular events: Northern Finland birth cohort follow-up
**Summary:** *ScienceDaily* summarizes a *BMC Cardiovascular Disorders* study tracking **3,231** individuals with wearable-measured sleep at age **46** and outcomes over **>10 years** of follow-up. The reported pattern is that highly irregular bedtimes—especially with **<8 hours** time in bed—are associated with a substantially higher risk of major cardiovascular events, with bedtime regularity emphasized over wake-time variability.
**Links:**
