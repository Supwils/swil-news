### 5. Harvard 30-Year Cohorts: 90–119 Min/Week Resistance Training Linked to 13% Lower All-Cause Mortality
**Summary:** Per **British Journal of Sports Medicine** online **June 2** and News-Medical, Harvard **T.H. Chan School of Public Health** researchers followed **147,374** participants (HPFS, NHS, NHS II) up to **30 years** with **35,798** deaths: vs. no resistance training, **90–119 min/week** was linked to **13%** lower all-cause mortality (HR **0.87**), **19%** lower cardiovascular mortality, and **27%** lower neurological mortality; **≥120 min/week** showed no extra benefit. Lowest mortality appeared with high aerobic volume (**≥45 MET-hours/week**) plus moderate resistance training (HR roughly **0.53–0.58**).

**Links:**

- [BJSM — Long-term resistance training with all-cause and cause-specific mortality (June 2, 2026)](https://bjsm.bmj.com/content/early/2026/05/28/bjsports-2025-110503)

**Commentary:** Large-scale evidence for how much strength work to pair with cardio; prioritize form and consistency over chasing extra minutes.

### 6. HUNT Study: Sustained Activity From Mid- to Late Adulthood May Cut Confirmed Sarcopenia Risk 78%
**Summary:** Per **BMC Geriatrics** published **June 4**, in Norway's **HUNT** cohort (**4,702** adults **70+**, mean age **76.4**), activity across three waves (**1984–2008**) per WHO 2020 guidelines was tracked: vs. persistent inactivity, being active at all three waves was linked to **78%** lower odds of **confirmed sarcopenia** (OR **0.22**); two and one active waves yielded **40%** and **26%** lower odds. **Becoming active** remained protective (OR **0.57**); **becoming inactive** did not.

**Links:**

- [BMC Geriatrics — Physical activity patterns from mid- to late adulthood and risk of sarcopenia (June 4, 2026)](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12877-026-07747-6)

**Commentary:** It's never too late to start moving; older adults should combine resistance and functional training to delay sarcopenia.

### 1. Aerobic Training May Improve Body Composition in Overweight/Obese Postmenopausal Women
**Summary:** Per **BMC Sports Science, Medicine and Rehabilitation** published **June 6**, a systematic review and dose–response meta-analysis of **16 RCTs** (**1,571** postmenopausal women with overweight/obesity) found vs. no-exercise controls, aerobic training averaged **−2.17 kg** body weight, **−0.73 kg/m²** BMI, **−1.40%** body fat, and **−2.02 cm** waist circumference; lean mass evidence was uncertain. Older baseline age predicted smaller weight/BMI change; no clear nonlinear dose–response for total aerobic volume.

**Links:**

- [BMC Sports Sci Med Rehabil — Impact of aerobic training on body composition in postmenopausal women (June 6, 2026)](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13102-026-01778-7)

**Commentary:** Evidence supports aerobic work for adiposity in this group; pair with resistance training to protect lean mass and track actual dose.
