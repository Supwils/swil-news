### 4. Forty-seven-year Swedish SPAF cohort: physical capacity trends from age 16 to 63
**Summary:** A Karolinska Institutet–led release summarized on ScienceDaily (May 15, 2026) describes the Swedish Physical Activity and Fitness (SPAF) study, with findings published in the *Journal of Cachexia, Sarcopenia and Muscle*. Hundreds of participants were followed longitudinally; fitness, strength, and muscular endurance begin to decline around age 35, with gradual worsening thereafter. Adults who increased physical activity later still improved performance by roughly 5–10% in the reported analyses. Authors caution that mechanisms and long-term clinical translation require continued follow-up.
**Links:**
- [ScienceDaily — A 47-year study reveals when strength and fitness start to fade](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/05/260515000947.htm)
- [Karolinska Institutet — Long-term study reveals physical ability peaks at age 35](https://news.ki.se/long-term-study-reveals-physical-ability-peaks-at-age-35)
**Commentary:** Treat the mid-30s as a public-health inflection point for building sustainable strength and cardiorespiratory reserve before sarcopenia- and falls-related trajectories steepen.

## II. Health & Medicine

### 5. Global renaming: PCOS becomes PMOS to reflect endocrine–metabolic complexity
**Summary:** The Endocrine Society and allied organizations announced that polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) will be renamed polyendocrine metabolic ovarian syndrome (PMOS), affecting more than 170 million women worldwide by the organizations’ estimates. Press materials argue the old label overemphasized “cysts” and underplayed hormonal, metabolic, mental-health, and reproductive dimensions. The renaming pathway is also described in *The Lancet*, with a multi-year education window ahead of fuller implementation in the 2028 international guideline cycle.
**Links:**
- [Endocrine Society — PMOS: New name to improve diagnosis and care](https://www.endocrine.org/news-and-advocacy/news-room/2026/pcos-name-change)
- [The Lancet — Name change commentary (full text)](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lancet/article/PIIS0140-6736(26)00717-8/fulltext)
**Commentary:** Naming shifts ripple through stigma, clinical pathways, and coding; over time they can align multimorbidity care (weight, mood, metabolism) under one evidence-based narrative.

## III. Nutrition & Diet

### 6. *Aging Cell*: four-week dietary shifts alter biological-age estimates in older adults
**Summary:** University of Sydney research summarized by ScienceDaily on May 12, 2026, randomized 104 adults aged 65–75 across four isonitrogenous diets. Groups farthest from baseline eating patterns showed reductions in biological-age markers within four weeks, with the strongest statistical signal in a lower-fat, higher-carbohydrate omnivorous pattern; the high-fat omnivorous arm closest to usual intake showed little change. Authors stress these are short-term biomarker shifts, not proof of durable disease-risk reduction, and call for larger, longer trials.
**Links:**
- [ScienceDaily — Scientists reversed biological age in older adults with a 4-week diet change](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/05/260511213144.htm)
- [Aging Cell — journal home (search DOI 10.1111/acel.70507)](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/journal/14749726)
**Commentary:** “Biological age” scores are useful for hypothesis generation and behavior science communication but should not be oversold as longevity guarantees in consumer marketing.

### 8. Warwick review: plant-based RCTs show lower CRP than omnivorous diets
**Summary:** Nutrition Insight reports a University of Warwick synthesis of seven randomized trials (541 participants) published in *Nutrition, Metabolism and Cardiovascular Diseases*, finding lower C-reactive protein on plant-based versus omnivorous patterns (mean reduction about 1.13 mg/L). Only seven trials met strict inclusion criteria from a much larger literature base. The story notes the largest inflammatory improvements appeared when plant-based diets co-occurred with structured exercise programs, though causal synergy still needs larger trials.
**Links:**
- [Nutrition Insight — Plant-based diets may lower inflammation vs omnivorous diets](https://www.nutritioninsight.com/news/plant-based-lowers-inflammation.html)
- [ScienceDirect — NMCD article](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0939475326000931)
**Commentary:** Low-grade inflammation intersects with overtraining risk and cardiometabolic health; this evidence supports testable “diet + movement” bundles rather than single-nutrient fixes.

### 9. *Scientific Reports*: weekend catch-up sleep and depressive symptoms in young adults (China & U.S.)
**Summary:** A 2026 *Scientific Reports* binational study analyzes survey data from China and the United States, modeling nonlinear relationships between weekend catch-up sleep and depressive symptoms. Abstract-level messaging emphasizes U-shaped patterns: both absent catch-up and excessive catch-up can associate with worse outcomes relative to moderate windows, with inflection points varying by country model specifications. Causal claims remain cautious, but the work informs behavioral counseling for irregular sleepers such as students and shift workers.
**Links:**
- [Scientific Reports — Weekend catch-up sleep and depressive symptoms (2026)](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-026-50383-8)
**Commentary:** Athletes and night-shift populations should evaluate catch-up sleep against total sleep debt and circadian phase goals, not only weekend hour totals.

**Summary:** A 2026 article in *Communications Psychology* links reduced slow-wave sleep to higher anxiety in older adults within a framework that connects nocturnal slow-wave activity to structural changes in emotion-processing circuitry. The clinical implication is that anxiety complaints in later life may co-exist with treatable sleep-architecture problems where insomnia-oriented behavioral therapies could have cross-diagnostic benefits.
**Links:**
- [Communications Psychology — Impaired slow-wave sleep and anxiety in aging](https://www.nature.com/articles/s44271-026-00401-2)
