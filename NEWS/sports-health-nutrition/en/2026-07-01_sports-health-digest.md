### 5. Exercise Science: 3–6 Daily Minutes of "Vigorous Intermittent Lifestyle Physical Activity" May Cut Mortality
**Summary:** Per Taiwanese health media on **July 1**, rehab physician **Wang Siheng** summarized **Nature Medicine** research tracking **25,000+** adults who self-reported no structured exercise while wearing activity monitors. Brief vigorous bursts in daily life — sprinting stairs, hurrying with heavy bags — were linked to lower mortality even without gym sessions. One burst per day showed a protective trend; **3** bursts of about **1** minute each (**3–6** minutes total) was associated with roughly **40%** lower all-cause mortality and nearly **50%** lower cardiovascular mortality. He recommended **30–60** seconds of stair sprints **3** times daily for those without cardiac disease, with intensity judged by needing two breaths before answering a question; people with heart conditions should consult a physician first.

**Links:**

- [SETN — 6 minutes of vigorous activity may cut mortality 40% (July 1, 2026)](https://www.setn.com/News.aspx?NewsID=1865123)
- [FTNN — Daily micro-burst exercise and mortality risk (July 1, 2026)](https://www.ftnn.com.tw/news/557504)

**Commentary:** No gym time is not the same as no benefit — turning stairs and commutes into controlled micro-sprints is one of the most practical prescriptions for busy adults.

### 6. Resistance Training "Sweet Spot": 90–120 Weekly Minutes May Optimize Mortality Reduction
**Summary:** Per BMJ Group and ScienceDaily, a **British Journal of Sports Medicine** study published in **June** followed **147,374** men and women for **30** years. **90–119** minutes of weekly resistance training was linked to **13%** lower all-cause mortality, with no further benefit above **120** minutes. That dose was especially tied to **19%** lower cardiovascular mortality and **27%** lower neurological-disease mortality; combining it with adequate aerobic activity cut all-cause mortality by up to **58%**. A separate **JACC** study (**June 17**) found women doing **≥2** hours of strength training plus **150** minutes of aerobic exercise had **45%** lower heart-attack risk.

**Links:**

- [BMJ Group — 90-120 weekly minutes of strength training may be optimal (June 2, 2026)](https://bmjgroup.com/90-120-weekly-minutes-of-strength-training-may-be-optimal-for-lowering-death-risk/)
- [Healthline — 2 hours strength training lowers heart disease risk in women (June 27, 2026)](https://www.healthline.com/health-news/strength-training-may-help-lower-heart-disease-risk-women)

**Commentary:** Resistance training need not be daily exhaustion; **2–3** sessions of **30–40** minutes plus brisk walking or cycling is a high-value longevity stack.

## II. Health & Medicine

### 7. The Lancet Review: Billions of mRNA Doses Confirm Safety and High Effectiveness
**Summary:** Per Euronews and UBC News (**June 30–July 1**), **The Lancet** published a global review led by University of British Columbia researchers integrating lab science, trials, and real-world surveillance across the mRNA vaccine lifecycle. Within **14–42** days post-vaccination, effectiveness was about **87%** against documented infection, **93%** against hospitalization, and **94%** against death; protection waned over time but boosters restored much of it. Myocarditis, pericarditis, and severe allergic reactions were very rare and lower than infection-related myocarditis risk. Authors urged expanding manufacturing and technology transfer in low- and middle-income countries and exploring influenza, RSV, and cancer applications.

### 8. Semaglutide and Tirzepatide: Observational Links to Fewer Anxiety and Depression Diagnoses in Obesity Care
**Summary:** Per News-Medical on **July 1**, a **Communications Medicine** study compared adults with obesity starting **semaglutide** or **tirzepatide** versus other weight-loss treatments. Both GLP-1 drugs were generally associated with lower hazards of anxiety disorders, cognitive deficits, mood disorders, and depression versus bariatric surgery; versus **naltrexone-bupropion**, similar patterns appeared among people with type 2 diabetes. Results varied by comparator, and tirzepatide showed higher anxiety and insomnia hazards than semaglutide among adults without diabetes. The design is observational and cannot prove direct psychiatric benefit.

### 12. Guangzhou Biobank 16-Year Follow-Up: Planetary Health Diet Tied to Mortality and Heart Age
**Summary:** Per **European Journal of Nutrition** (**May 26**), analysis of **25,550** participants aged **≥50** in the Guangzhou Biobank Cohort Study with **417,590** person-years of follow-up found each **10-point** increase in Planetary Health Diet score linked to **6%** lower all-cause mortality (HR **0.94**) and **8%** lower cardiovascular mortality (HR **0.92**), with no significant cancer association. White blood cell count and waist-to-hip ratio partly mediated the links. In women, higher PHD scores correlated with lower "heart age"; no significant association appeared in men. PHD emphasizes whole grains, produce, legumes, and nuts while limiting red meat and ultra-processed foods.

**Links:**

- [European Journal of Nutrition — Planetary health diet and mortality in GBCS (May 26, 2026)](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00394-026-04002-x)

**Commentary:** A diet balancing personal and planetary health shows cardiovascular signals in Chinese older adults; sex differences may reflect hormonal and body-composition factors needing further study.

## IV. Lifestyle

### 13. APA Feature: Bidirectional Sleep–Mental Health Links; CBT-I Remains Gold Standard for Insomnia
**Summary:** Per **APA Monitor** in **June**, growing evidence shows sleep disorders and depression, anxiety, PTSD, and bipolar disorder often co-occur bidirectionally — sometimes with sleep problems preceding psychiatric illness. Insomnia raises depression risk roughly **10-fold** and anxiety **17-fold** versus the general population; interventions that improve sleep, especially **cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia (CBT-I)**, also ease depression and anxiety. The article highlights consistent bed/wake times, avoiding long daytime naps, and reducing pre-sleep screen use; persistent fatigue despite hygiene changes warrants evaluation for dozens of clinical sleep disorders rather than self-medicating.

**Links:**

- [APA Monitor — The new science of sleep (June 1, 2026)](https://www.apa.org/monitor/2026/06/sleep-brain-mental-health)

**Commentary:** Athletes whose recovery lags despite higher training load should audit sleep quality before adding more caffeine; CBT-I matches or beats drugs for chronic insomnia with longer-lasting effects.

### 14. Large U.S. Data: Both Short and Long Sleep Linked to Worse Depression and Overall Health
**Summary:** Per **PLOS One** (**2026**), a weighted analysis of **318,000** U.S. adults found baseline depression prevalence about **39.5%** among those with recommended sleep (**6–8** hours). Sleep **≤5** hours was linked to a **14.1** percentage-point higher depression incidence; **≥9** hours to **12.9** points higher. Short sleepers reported **5.3** poor mental-health days and **4.4** poor physical-health days per month on average; long sleepers also fared worse. A **BMC Psychiatry** (**2026**) NHANES study of **24,703** adults found a **U-shaped** link between sleep duration and suicidal ideation; sleep difficulty independently raised ideation risk **2.67-fold**, with significant interaction when long sleep co-occurred with sleep difficulty.

**Links:**

- [PLOS One — Sleep duration and depression in U.S. adults (2026)](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0321347)
- [BMC Psychiatry — Sleep duration and difficulty on suicidal ideation (2026)](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12888-026-07817-4)

**Commentary:** More sleep is not always better; adults should aim for **7–9** hours with fewer night awakenings — chronic hypersomnia or insomnia can both signal broader health problems.
