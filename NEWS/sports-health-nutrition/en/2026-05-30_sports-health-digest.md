### 1. Review: Poor Sleep May Unify Dementia Risk Factors Through Glymphatic Clearance
**Summary:** Per Medical News Today and University of Rochester Medicine on May 30, a May 26 *Science* review by Maiken Nedergaard argues that chronic stress, depression, cardiovascular disease, and aging may converge by disrupting sleep-dependent glymphatic waste clearance. Recommendations include regular schedules, sufficient sleep, exercise, and limiting evening stimulants; heart-rate variability during sleep is discussed as a potential biomarker.

### 3. Study: Some People Control HIV Off Medication via Autologous Antibodies and T Cells
**Summary:** Per The Brighter Side of News on May 30, Aarhus University Hospital and collaborators tracked three "post-intervention controllers" (PICs): two maintained viral loads below 20 copies/mL for more than 6.5 and 7.5 years after stopping antiretroviral therapy (ART). Potent autologous neutralizing antibodies and polyfunctional HIV-specific T cells persisted before and during ART interruption. Findings published in *Nature Immunology* (March 2026) may inform strategies to strengthen existing immune responses rather than rely solely on lifelong ART.

**Links:**

- [The Brighter Side of News — How some people control HIV without daily medication (May 30, 2026)](https://www.thebrighterside.news/post/scientists-discover-how-some-people-control-hiv-without-daily-medication/)
- [Nature Immunology — Autologous neutralizing antibodies and polyfunctional T cells](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1038/s41590-026-02448-z)

**Commentary:** If durable responses are confirmed at scale, HIV care could shift — but individual variation is wide; case reports must not be read as a replicable cure protocol.

## III. Nutrition & Diet

### 1. Nearly One Million Adults Across Six Cohorts: Anti-Inflammatory Diet Pattern Tied to ~16% Lower Colorectal Cancer Risk
**Summary:** Per Earth.com on May 30, Fred K. Tabung (Ohio State University) harmonized diet data from six U.S. and European cohorts (~1 million adults, ~15 years follow-up). Those closest to an anti-inflammatory pattern had ~16% lower colorectal cancer risk versus the most pro-inflammatory pattern; insulin-friendly and overall diet-quality scores showed similar protection (~18% and ~20%). Benefits tracked vegetables, whole fruit, legumes, and nuts — not single "superfoods." Published in the *American Journal of Clinical Nutrition*.

**Links:**

- [Earth.com — Anti-inflammatory diet pattern and colorectal cancer risk (May 30, 2026)](https://www.earth.com/news/no-superfood-needed-a-large-study-links-lower-colorectal-cancer-risk-to-an-anti-inflammatory-diet-pattern/)

**Commentary:** Cross-continent consistency strengthens the case for overall dietary patterns over product hype; gradual swaps beat crash diets.

### 2. NutriNet-Santé: Eight Common Preservatives Linked to Higher Hypertension and CVD Risk
**Summary:** Per the European Society of Cardiology and CNN (May 20–28), an analysis of 112,395 French NutriNet-Santé volunteers followed 7–8 years found highest non-antioxidant preservative intake associated with ~29% higher hypertension risk and ~16% higher cardiovascular disease risk. Eight of 17 commonly consumed preservatives were linked to hypertension, including potassium sorbate, sodium nitrite, citric acid, and ascorbic acid; ascorbic acid was also tied to CVD. Published in the *European Heart Journal* (May 20); observational design.

**Links:**

### 3. Meta-Analysis: ~170 g Legumes and 60–80 g Soy Daily Linked to ~28%–30% Lower Hypertension Risk
**Summary:** Per ScienceDaily and Earth.com in May, a UK–Norway team pooled 12 prospective studies (~300,000 adults) in *BMJ Nutrition, Prevention & Health*: highest legume intake linked to ~16% lower hypertension risk versus lowest intake; highest soy intake ~19% lower. Dose-response curves peaked around 170 g/day of legumes and 60–80 g/day of soy (~1 cup cooked beans, 2–3 oz tofu), with ~28%–30% risk reduction; no extra soy benefit beyond that range. Authors graded the evidence as "probable" causality.

**Links:**

- [ScienceDaily — Eating more beans and soy could slash high blood pressure risk (May 26, 2026)](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/05/260523103906.htm)
- [Earth.com — Beans and soy may lower high blood pressure risk (May 2026)](https://www.earth.com/news/beans-and-soy-may-lower-high-blood-pressure-risk-but-the-amount-matters/)

**Commentary:** Legumes and soy are cheap, accessible cardio-friendly proteins — but benefits track dose within an overall diet, not unlimited stacking.

**Summary:** Per ScienceAlert and Springer Nature in May, a University of Coimbra review of dozens of studies suggests ketone bodies from a high-fat, low-carb diet may supply alternative brain fuel, reduce inflammation, and promote autophagy — with supportive signals in Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, and related animal models. Most evidence is preclinical; authors call for more clinical trials on safety and long-term use. Published in *Translational Neurodegeneration* (May 25).

**Links:**

- [ScienceAlert — Keto diet may help protect against multiple brain diseases](https://www.sciencealert.com/keto-diet-may-help-protect-against-multiple-brain-diseases-study-finds)

### 2. UK MHRA Warns: Heat Can Degrade Medicines; Insulin Needs Particular Care
**Summary:** Per GOV.UK and The Mirror in May, the Medicines and Healthcare products Regulatory Agency reminded the public that most medicines should be stored below 25°C — cars, sunny windowsills, and all-day bags can degrade tablets, inhalers, insulin, and EpiPens. Some blood-pressure, diuretic, and diabetes drugs may also raise dehydration or heat-illness risk. Guidance: keep medicines cool and dry, use insulated bags when traveling, and watch for color, smell, or texture changes.

**Links:**

- [GOV.UK — Don't let this heatwave affect your medicines: MHRA tips](https://www.gov.uk/government/news/dont-let-this-heatwave-affect-your-medicines-three-important-tips-from-the-mhra)
- [The Mirror — Warning for people taking tablets, inhalers, insulin or EpiPens](https://www.mirror.co.uk/news/health/warning-people-taking-tablets-inhalers-37206301)

**Commentary:** Summer sports and travel plans should include medication storage alongside personal heat safety — especially for people managing chronic conditions.

**Summary:** Per mindbodygreen on May 30, a prospective study found severe short-term sleep restriction without recovery sleep linked to higher all-cause mortality, while those who quickly "rebounded" with recovery sleep did not show the same elevated risk. Authors still rank consistent adequate sleep as the gold standard; catch-up sleep is a mitigation tactic, not a long-term plan.

**Links:**
