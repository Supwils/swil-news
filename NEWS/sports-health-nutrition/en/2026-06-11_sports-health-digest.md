### 5. Harvard 30-year cohort: 90–119 min/week of resistance training linked to 13% lower all-cause mortality
**Summary:** Per the *British Journal of Sports Medicine* and Medical Xpress in **June**, Harvard's **Yiwen Zhang** and colleagues analyzed **147,374** people with up to **30 years** of follow-up: versus no resistance training, **90–119 minutes/week** was associated with **13%** lower all-cause mortality (HR **0.87**), **19%** lower cardiovascular death, and **27%** lower neurological disease death; no further benefit was seen above **120 min/week**. Joint analyses showed lowest mortality with high aerobic activity (~**≥45 MET-hours/week**) plus moderate resistance training (HRs roughly **0.53–0.58**). The study is observational; some exercise data were self-reported.

**Links:**

- [PubMed — Long-term resistance training with all-cause and cause-specific mortality (BJSM, 2026)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42230125/)
- [Medical Xpress — 90–120 weekly minutes of strength training may be optimal (June 2026)](https://medicalxpress.com/news/2026-06-analysis-weekly-minutes-strength-optimal.html)

**Commentary:** "Adequate strength + sufficient cardio" beats blind volume escalation; ~**1.5–2 hours/week** of lifting may already approach the benefit plateau for most adults.

## II. Health & Medicine

### 1. FDA approves first new sunscreen active in 25+ years: bemotrizinol (Parsol Shield)
**Summary:** Per ScienceAlert, Healio, and EWG on **June 10–11**, the U.S. FDA added **bemotrizinol (BEMT)** to the OTC sunscreen monograph as **Parsol Shield** — the first new chemical UV filter approved in the U.S. in over **25 years**. It provides broad UVA/UVB protection with high photostability, is **GRASE** for adults and children **6 months+** at up to **6%**, and has low systemic absorption. Applicant dsm-firmenich holds **18 months** of marketing exclusivity; products are expected soon. The ingredient has been used internationally for **20+ years**.

**Links:**

### 2. University of Florida study: glucosamine supplements may be linked to faster Alzheimer's progression
**Summary:** Per UF Health and *Nature Metabolism* on **June 9**, UF's **Ramon Sun** team proposes **hyperglycosylation** as a metabolic driver in Alzheimer's disease (AD). A retrospective analysis of de-identified UF Health records (**2012–2024**) found ~**8%** of AD and mild cognitive impairment (MCI) patients reported glucosamine use; after adjustment, MCI users had ~**25%** higher odds of progressing to dementia, and established AD/related dementia patients had ~**25%** higher mortality risk. Mouse experiments showed oral glucosamine worsened cognition in AD models. Authors stress prospective trials are needed; causation is not proven.

**Links:**

## III. Nutrition & Diet

### 1. RCT: high-linoleic diet lowers EPA and shifts lipid mediators toward pro-inflammatory n-6 profile
**Summary:** Per NutraIngredients on **June 11**, U.S. researchers in *Nutrients* ran a **12-week** randomized trial in **52** healthy adults on low-LA (**2.5%** of energy) vs. high-LA (**10.0%**) diets. The high-LA arm showed marked reductions in plasma **EPA** and EPA-derived oxylipins, higher **ARA/EPA** ratios, and a more **n-6-dominant** inflammatory mediator profile; **ARA** and **DHA** did not differ significantly. Authors note modern Western diets — rich in soybean and corn oils — may alter fatty-acid competition and inflammatory signaling.

**Links:**

- [NutraIngredients — Study provides insight into metabolic consequences of vegetable oils (June 11, 2026)](https://www.nutraingredients.com/Article/2026/06/11/study-provides-insight-into-metabolic-consequences-of-vegetable-oils/)

**Commentary:** Oil quality affects n-3/n-6 balance; swapping high–omega-6 seed oils for olive oil and adding fatty fish can support a healthier lipid mediator profile.

Authors still warn many UPFs are nutritionally poor but caution against blaming "the factory" simplistically.

### 3. Study argues safer alcohol guidance should cap at one standard drink per day
**Summary:** Per News-Medical on **June 11**, an analysis in the *Journal of Studies on Alcohol and Drugs* found no net protective health benefit even at low-moderate intake; absolute risk rises with volume. At **14 drinks/week** for men (a former U.S. guideline ceiling), absolute alcohol-attributable death risk was ~**1 in 25 (4%)**; women's relative cirrhosis risk at that level (**5.38**) far exceeded men's (**2.10**). Binge patterns also raised breast cancer, cardiovascular, and injury risks. Authors recommend revising guidance to **≤1 standard drink/day** for current drinkers, plus labeling and screening.

**Links:**

- [News-Medical — One drink a day may be the new upper limit for safer alcohol guidance (June 11, 2026)](https://www.news-medical.net/news/20260611/One-drink-a-day-may-be-the-new-upper-limit-for-safer-alcohol-guidance.aspx)

**Commentary:** The "moderate drinking is healthy" narrative keeps weakening; social drinking should stay minimal, especially in pregnancy and chronic disease.

### 1. International team urges elevating sleep as a global public health priority
**Summary:** Per Medical Xpress in **June**, researchers including Forschungszentrum Jülich argue in a perspective that ~**one-third** of the global population has sleep problems; poor sleep costs five industrialized nations up to ~**$680 billion/year** in absenteeism, lost productivity, and healthcare.
