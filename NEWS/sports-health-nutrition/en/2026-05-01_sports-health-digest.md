### 2. Exercise *variety*—not just total volume—linked to lower premature mortality in long-term cohorts
**Summary:** A study in *BMJ Medicine* analyzing more than **111,000** adults across the Nurses’ Health Study and Health Professionals Follow-up Study ( **30+** years of follow-up) reports that, after accounting for overall activity, people reporting the **broadest mix** of activity types had about a **19%** lower risk of all-cause mortality, with additional risk reductions across major cause-specific deaths. The work is **observational** and relies on **self-reported** activity. Harvard Chan School and ScienceDaily communicated the main implications for the public.
**Sources:**
- [ScienceDaily — This one change to your exercise routine could add years to your life](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/04/260426012305.htm)
- [Harvard Chan — Exercise variety—not just amount—linked to lower risk of premature mortality](https://hsph.harvard.edu/news/exercise-variety-not-just-amount-linked-to-lower-risk-of-premature-mortality/)
**Commentary:** Practical takeaway for coaching: thoughtfully rotate modalities within safety limits instead of endlessly stacking volume on a single modality.

## III. Nutrition & Diet

### 6. Higher ultra-processed food intake linked to worse attention and higher dementia-risk scores (Australia)
**Summary:** CNN’s **April 29, 2026** piece covers a study in *Alzheimer's & Dementia: Diagnosis, Assessment & Disease Monitoring* following **>2,100** Australians aged **40–70** with food diaries and cognitive tests. Authors report that each **~10 percentage-point** rise in energy from ultra-processed foods associates with lower attention/processing-speed test performance and higher scores on a validated **20-year dementia risk** tool; associations persist after accounting for plant-rich patterns, underscoring processing—not just “healthy vs unhealthy” labels. The study is **observational**.
**Sources:**
- [CNN — Even small amounts of ultraprocessed foods increase risk for dementia, study says](https://www.cnn.com/2026/04/29/health/ultraprocessed-food-dementia-study-wellness)
**Commentary:** Reinforces that occasional UPF “add-ons” may still matter for brain health over decades.

### 7. Chronic stress plus late-night calories: higher odds of bowel symptoms, lower microbiome diversity
**Summary:** A **May 1, 2026** ScienceDaily brief summarizes work scheduled for presentation at **Digestive Disease Week 2026**. Analyzing **>11,000** NHANES respondents, researchers report that people with high stress who consume **>25%** of daily calories after **9 p.m.** had **~1.7×** the odds of constipation/diarrhea vs. lower-stress, earlier eaters; a **~4,000-person** American Gut Project analysis found a **~2.5×** symptom excess plus reduced microbial diversity among those combining both exposures. Findings remain **associational**, and conference abstracts precede peer-reviewed publication.
**Sources:**
- [ScienceDaily — Your gut takes a “double hit” from stress and late-night eating](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/04/260429102026.htm)
**Commentary:** “Chrononutrition” is a lever stress-management and GI clinics can jointly discuss—especially for shift workers.

### 9. UC Berkeley clinicians scale transdiagnostic sleep & circadian care for mental-health clients
**Summary:** Berkeley News’ **April 16, 2026** podcast feature describes the Transdiagnostic Sleep and Circadian Intervention (**TSC**) co-developed by Professor Allison Harvey—combining standardized wake times, reduced pre-bedscreen exposure, and other behavioral tools.
