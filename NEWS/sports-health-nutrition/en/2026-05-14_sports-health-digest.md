## I. Sports & Exercise

### 1. ACSM publishes updated resistance-training Position Stand: consistency beats “perfect programming”
**Summary:** The American College of Sports Medicine released a major Position Stand in *Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise*, synthesizing 137 systematic reviews spanning more than 30,000 participants. The central message is that the largest gains come from moving from no resistance training to regular participation; training all major muscle groups at least twice per week matters more than chasing highly complex periodization. Non-traditional modalities—bands, bodyweight work, and home-based routines—are emphasized as effective when individualized for goals and safety.
**Links:**
- [Newswise — ACSM Unveils Landmark 2026 Resistance Training Guidelines](https://www.newswise.com/articles/acsm-unveils-landmark-2026-resistance-training-guidelines-first-update-in-17-years)
- [Medicine & Science in Sports & Exercise — ACSM Position Stand (journal)](https://journals.lww.com/acsm-msse/fulltext/2026/04000/american_college_of_sports_medicine_position.21.aspx)
**Commentary:** For general fitness and rehabilitation messaging, prioritizing adherence-friendly prescriptions should reduce “program perfectionism” and improve long-term uptake.

### 3. “Snack breaks” from sitting: frequent short walking breaks and post-meal glycemic control
**Summary:** A China News Service health article (May 12, 2026) summarizes trial evidence that interrupting prolonged sitting with brief walking breaks—on the order of two minutes every ~20 minutes—can materially lower post-meal glucose and insulin dynamics compared with continuous sitting. The piece also references separate work on brief movement snacks every ~30 minutes for musculoskeletal comfort and cautions against uninterrupted sitting beyond roughly ~90 minutes even for people who exercise later in the day.
**Links:**
- [China News Service — Break up sitting to improve metabolism](https://www.chinanews.com.cn/jk/2026/05-12/10619915.shtml)
**Commentary:** Embedding frequent low-threshold movement into desk-bound routines is a pragmatic lever for cardiometabolic health alongside structured workouts.

### 5. Oral GLP-1 therapy after stopping injections: orforglipron maintenance trial draws coverage
**Summary:** BBC reporting summarizes a manufacturer-funded *Nature Medicine* trial in which adults who had lost weight on GLP-1 injections discontinued injections and were randomized to daily oral orforglipron versus placebo for one year: the active group retained more than ~70% of prior weight loss versus roughly ~38–50% in placebo; gastrointestinal side effects were common but mostly mild. Experts emphasize longer-term durability questions and highlight pills as a potential adherence advantage for some patients.
**Links:**
- [BBC News — Daily pill helps keep weight off after stopping obesity jabs](https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cwy2e9wlvjxo)
- [Nature Medicine — Trial article (DOI hub)](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-026-04386-7)
**Commentary:** As obesity is framed medically as chronic and relapsing, sustainable strategies after discontinuing injectables are as consequential as induction-phase weight loss.

## III. Nutrition

### 8. Four-week dietary remodeling and biological-age-related biomarker shifts (older adults)
**Summary:** A University of Sydney trial summarized via ScienceDaily (study publicity dated May 12, 2026; journal *Aging Cell*) randomized adults aged 65–75 among four isoprotein (~14% energy) diets differing in fat/carbohydrate balance and animal versus plant protein emphasis. Compared with a high-fat omnivorous pattern mirroring prior habits (minimal change), three alternative arms showed favorable shifts in composite biomarker profiles tied to biological aging estimates within four weeks, strongest statistically for a lower-fat, higher-carbohydrate omnivorous pattern (~53% carbohydrate; ~28–29% fat). Authors caution on generalization beyond short horizons.
**Links:**
- [ScienceDaily — University of Sydney aging-related dietary biomarker trial](https://www.sciencedaily.com/releases/2026/05/260511213144.htm)
**Commentary:** The rapid biomarker responsiveness is hypothesis-generating for late-life nutrition transitions but not yet a stand-alone basis for population dietary mandates.

### 9. Plant-based patterns vs omnivory: RCT umbrella synthesis reports larger CRP reductions
**Summary:** Nutrition Insight covers a University of Warwick systematic synthesis in *Nutrition, Metabolism and Cardiovascular Diseases*: among seven randomized trials (541 participants), plant-based dietary patterns reduced C-reactive protein by about 1.13 mg/L versus omnivorous comparators on average, with larger effects when coupled with structured exercise. Authors note remaining scarcity of very large trials.
**Links:**
- [Nutrition Insight — Plant-based diets and inflammation evidence review](https://www.nutritioninsight.com/news/plant-based-lowers-inflammation.html)
**Commentary:** Pairing dietary pattern shifts with physical activity may amplify anti-inflammatory pathways relevant to cardiometabolic prevention.

### 10. Time-restricted eating versus calorie counting in PCOS: Nature Medicine RCT highlights
**Summary:** Northwestern University communications (May 12, 2026) describe a three-arm, six-month randomized trial (*Nature Medicine*) of 76 women with PCOS comparing (i) 6-hour time-restricted feeding (13:00–19:00) without calorie tracking, (ii) ~25% calorie restriction, and (iii) control. Percent weight loss at six months was approximately −4.32% (time-restricted) and −4.66% (calorie restriction) versus control; HbA1c declined in the time-restricted arm; total testosterone decreased in both intervention arms; no serious adverse events were reported.
**Links:**
- [Northwestern University Feinberg School of Medicine — PCOS time-restricted eating trial](https://news.feinberg.northwestern.edu/2026/05/12/time-restricted-eating-supports-weight-loss-in-women-with-pcos/)
- [Nature Medicine — Trial article (DOI hub)](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-026-04316-7)
**Commentary:** For patients fatigued by daily calorie tracking, time-restricted protocols—when medically appropriate—may offer an evidence-backed alternative within obesity–hyperandrogenism care pathways.
