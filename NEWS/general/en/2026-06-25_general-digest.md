## I. Global Headlines

### 1. Twin earthquakes in Venezuela kill at least 235
**Summary:** According to AP News, BBC, CBS News, and CNN on June 25, northern coastal La Guaira was struck on the evening of June 24 by back-to-back magnitude 7.2 and 7.5 earthquakes—among Venezuela's strongest in more than a century—with shaking felt across the Caribbean. Health Minister Carlos Alvarado said at least 235 people were killed and more than 4,300 injured, with the toll expected to rise; an opposition-run missing-persons site briefly listed more than 46,000 people unaccounted for. Roughly 250 buildings were damaged or destroyed, including a 10-story hotel reduced to rubble; Simón Bolívar International Airport was severely damaged and closed, and schools were shut for several days. Interim President Delcy Rodríguez declared a state of emergency and a $200 million reconstruction fund. Mexico, Qatar, Brazil, Spain, and others sent search teams and supplies. Secretary of State Marco Rubio said the U.S. was immediately deploying rescue teams and medical aid at President Trump's direction; the State Department pledged $150 million in assistance, and Treasury partially waived sanctions through October 23 to facilitate relief transactions.

### 2. Iran drone strike on Hormuz merchant ship; UN pauses seafarer evacuation

The attack came just one week after the U.S. and Iran signed a 60-day memorandum of understanding on June 17 pledging Iran's "best efforts" for toll-free safe passage. IMO Secretary-General Dominguez said a large-scale evacuation of more than 11,000 stranded seafarers, announced June 24, would be paused pending clarity. The IRGC warned that ships must use only Tehran-approved routes or forfeit safety guarantees—directly conflicting with a temporary corridor coordinated by Oman and the IMO. Visiting Bahrain, Secretary Rubio said Washington would judge Iran by whether ships actually move, not rhetoric, and reiterated opposition to any Hormuz transit fees.

### 11. Ethnic unity promotion law takes effect July 1; extraterritorial clause draws scrutiny
**Summary:** According to RFA and Bannedbook (citing the June 24 State Council Information Office briefing) on June 25, China's Law on the Promotion of Ethnic Unity and Progress takes effect July 1—the only new law since the 18th Party Congress with a standalone preamble. Vice Minister of Justice Hu Weilie said June 24 the clause aligns with jurisdiction principles, targets unlawful overseas conduct, and will not affect normal cultural, academic, or business exchange; he called Western media portrayals distortions. Legal scholar Liu Yaoming told RFA extraterritorial enforcement lacks legal legitimacy and may deter overseas critics.
