### 2. Venezuela Earthquake Death Toll Rises to About 1,450; Rescue Continues Past 72-Hour Window
**Summary:** According to Reuters/MarketScreener and News18 on June 29, five days after twin 7.2- and 7.5-magnitude quakes struck central Venezuela on the night of June 24, National Assembly President Jorge Rodriguez said on June 28 that the death toll had reached 1,450, with 3,150 injured, 12,721 displaced, and 774 buildings collapsed; Acting President Delcy Rodriguez announced a presidential commission to assess building habitability. The U.S. Geological Survey said the twin quakes could rank among Latin America's deadliest in a century.

### 3. Europe Faces Worst Recorded Heatwave; Excess Deaths Exceed 1,300
**Summary:** According to DW, the WMO, News18, and UN News on June 29, an extreme heatwave that began around June 21 continued across Western, Central, and Southern Europe, with multiple countries setting historic or June records: Coschen, Germany, hit 41.7°C on June 28 (a national record); Słubice, Poland, 40.5°C (breaking a 105-year record); Doksany, Czech Republic, 41.9°C; Pissos, France, 44.3°C; Paris 40.9°C (a June record); southern England 37.3°C on June 26. The WHO said on June 28 that more than 1,300 excess deaths linked to high temperatures had been recorded in Europe since June 21; French health authorities reported roughly 1,000 excess deaths from June 24–27 with the figure still likely to rise. An estimated 150–200 million people faced temperatures above 35°C; rail speed limits, power-grid strain, and farm losses mounted as Germany, Belgium, Italy, Switzerland, the Netherlands, and others issued red heat alerts. The WMO cited an "Omega Block" pattern plus human-driven climate change making such events more frequent and intense.

## II. U.S. Politics

### 5. Supreme Court Overturns Humphrey's Executor 6–3; President May Fire Independent Agency Members for Policy Reasons
**Summary:** According to ABC News, SCOTUSblog, and NBC News on June 29, the Supreme Court ruled 6–3 in Trump v. Slaughter, overturning the 1935 precedent Humphrey's Executor v. United States and holding that a federal law barring the president from firing Federal Trade Commission members except for cause is unconstitutional; the president may remove FTC Democrat Rebecca Slaughter and similar officials over policy disagreements. Chief Justice John Roberts wrote for the majority that roughly two dozen multi-member independent agencies Congress designed to be insulated from politics no longer enjoy for-cause removal protection. Trump called the ruling on Truth Social one of "the most important ever given with respect to Presidential Powers." Critics warn the SEC, FCC, FEC, and others could be packed along partisan lines; supporters say it restores political accountability over the bureaucracy. The ruling does not abolish the agencies but fundamentally changes how independently they operate.

**Links:**

- [SCOTUSblog — Court allows Trump to fire FTC commissioner and overturns major restraint on presidential power](https://www.scotusblog.com/2026/06/court-allows-trump-to-fire-ftc-commissioner-and-overturns-major-restraint-on-presidential-power/)
- [ABC News — Supreme Court upholds Trump firing of FTC commissioner](https://abcnews.com/Politics/supreme-court-allows-trump-firing-ftc-commissioner/story?id=129203395)

**Commentary:** A 91-year administrative-law cornerstone overturned in a day marks the conservative court tilting sharply toward presidential power over independent regulation—personnel upheaval across agencies will land faster than academic debate.

### 6. Supreme Court Blocks Trump from Firing Fed Governor Lisa Cook 5–4, Preserving Central Bank Independence
**Summary:** According to NBC News, ABC News, and Reuters on June 29, in a separate 5–4 ruling released the same day, the Court denied Trump's request to immediately remove Federal Reserve Governor Lisa Cook, leaving a lower-court injunction in place so she can remain on the board while litigation continues. Roberts wrote that although the president may remove Fed governors "for cause" under the Federal Reserve Act, Trump failed to afford Cook the statutory procedural protections, so this dismissal was invalid; the ruling did not decide the underlying mortgage-fraud allegations, leaving room for future legal challenges. Cook was fired in August 2025 over mortgage-fraud allegations raised by a Trump appointee; she called it a pretext for monetary-policy differences. The Court found the Fed distinct because of its unique structure and a tradition since 1913 in which no president had tried to remove a governor for cause. Paired with Slaughter, the twin rulings signal expanded presidential power over regulators but a procedural barrier at the monetary-policy "last redoubt."
